# RFC 9591: The FROST Protocol (Two-Round Threshold Schnorr)

**Authors**: D. Connolly (Zcash Foundation), C. Komlo (U. Waterloo / ZF), I. Goldberg (U. Waterloo), C. A. Wood (Cloudflare).
**Venue**: IRTF Crypto Forum Research Group (CFRG), Informational, June 2024. (Underlying academic paper: Komlo & Goldberg, "FROST", SAC 2020, eprint 2020/852.)

## Key findings

- FROST is a **two-round** protocol: **Round 1 = commitment phase** — each participant generates secret nonces and publishes public nonce commitments to the Coordinator. This round is **message-independent, so it can be precomputed / batched in advance**; **Round 2 = signing phase** — given the message and the commitment list, participants compute signature shares.
- Each participant generates **two nonces per signing operation**: a **hiding nonce** and a **binding nonce**. Binding factors are computed from the participant commitments to prevent post-hoc (adaptive) manipulation — this is FROST's defense against ROS/Wagner-type forgeries.
- Genuine **t-of-n threshold** scheme (`MIN_PARTICIPANTS ≤ NUM_PARTICIPANTS ≤ MAX_PARTICIPANTS`): any subset of at least the threshold can sign. This is the fundamental contrast with MuSig2's **n-of-n**.
- **Coordinator role**: selects signers, routes messages between the two rounds, aggregates signature shares, and validates the final signature. The Coordinator is **not trusted with private information** and operates over **authenticated (not necessarily confidential)** channels.
- Security target is **EUF-CMA**. FROST is **explicitly NOT robust**: "all participants are required to complete the protocol honestly in order to generate a valid signature." The RFC points to ROAST as the wrapper that adds robustness.

## What this source contributes

The canonical, citable standard for FROST's round/commitment structure, its Coordinator model, and its acknowledged lack of robustness — the backbone of the MuSig2-vs-FROST comparison. Shows that FROST *keeps* a commitment round (folded into a precomputable preprocessing phase) where MuSig2 removes it via the two-nonce trick, though both defend the same concurrent-security concern.
