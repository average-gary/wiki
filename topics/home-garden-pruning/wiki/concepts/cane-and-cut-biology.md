# Cane and Cut Biology

Pruning decisions follow the plant's growth biology. Three patterns cover everything in this wiki.

## 1. Biennial cane (brambles)

Both raspberries and blackberries grow biennial canes:

```
Year 1:  Primocane (vegetative, green/fleshy bark)
              ↓ overwinters
Year 2:  Floricane (fruiting, brown/woody bark)
              ↓ after harvest
         dies → cut to ground
```

**Implication**: The cane that fruits this year was last year's primocane. Removing primocanes mid-year sacrifices next year's fruit. Removing floricanes after harvest is sanitation, not loss.

Exceptions:
- **Primocane-fruiting raspberries** (Heritage, Caroline) and primocane-fruiting blackberries (Prime-Ark series) fruit in fall on the *current year's* primocane tips. Mow-down each spring works because you're betting on this year's primocanes for fall fruit.
- **Black/purple raspberries** form stationary "hills" rather than suckering — but the cane biology is the same.

See [[../topics/blackberry-pruning|Blackberry pruning]] and [[../topics/raspberry-pruning|Raspberry pruning]].

## 2. Cane renewal (blueberries, shrub roses)

Multi-stem shrubs that don't have a biennial cycle still benefit from cane-by-cane age management:

- Each cane has a **productive lifespan** — roughly 4-8 years for blueberries, 3-5 years for shrub roses.
- Productivity peaks in the middle (year 2-4 for berries, year 1-2 for roses) and declines as the cane ages.
- **Renewal pruning** removes the oldest canes annually so the bush stays in a productive age mix.
- Replacement canes break from the crown or from latent buds on stub-cut older canes.

**General rule**: remove ~20% of canes per year, or roughly the oldest 1-3 canes, leaving 6-10 canes of mixed ages.

See [[cane-renewal|Cane renewal pruning]] for the full protocol.

## 3. Tree architecture (cherries)

Trees have a permanent woody structure (trunk + scaffolds) that you train once during establishment, then maintain. Pruning operates at three time scales:

| Scale | What you cut | When |
|-------|-------------|------|
| **Architectural** | Establish scaffolds, leader, branch angles | Years 1-3 |
| **Renewal** | ~20% of fruiting wood per year on a 5-7 year cycle | Mature, annually |
| **Maintenance** | Dead, diseased, crossing, water sprouts, suckers | Anytime (preferably summer for cherry) |

**Apical dominance**: The terminal bud at the top of each shoot suppresses lateral buds via auxin. Heading cuts remove apical dominance and force lateral branching. Thinning cuts (removing entire shoots) preserve apical dominance and don't force regrowth at the cut.

For sweet cherry specifically, vigor and apical dominance are extreme — without active training, the tree forms one tall whip with sparse scaffolds. See [[../topics/cherry-pruning|Cherry pruning]] and [[../reference/training-systems-cherry|Cherry training systems]].

## Heading cuts vs. thinning cuts

Two cut types, two different effects:

### Heading cut
Cuts a shoot back to a bud or short stub. **Forces branching** below the cut. Use for:
- Tipping bramble primocanes (forces lateral branching)
- Heading nursery whips at planting
- Shortening floricane laterals on blackberries
- Shortening rose canes during hard prune

### Thinning cut
Removes an entire shoot at its origin (back to the trunk, scaffold, or ground). **No branching response.** Opens the canopy. Use for:
- Removing crossing or rubbing branches
- Cane renewal in blueberries and roses
- Removing spent floricanes
- Removing whole water sprouts

A pruning session typically uses both: thinning cuts for structural shape, heading cuts for branching where you need it.

## Why timing matters

Most temperate pruning is done **late winter / early spring** when:

- Plants are dormant — minimal energy lost from cuts
- Wood is visible without leaves — easier to see structure
- Wounds heal quickly as growth resumes
- No risk of stimulating tender new growth that frosts

**Major exception**: sweet cherry. Dormant cuts on cherry are vectors for bacterial canker (PSS) and Cytospora. Late-July post-harvest pruning reduces canker progression ~5x (Carroll et al. 2010, Cornell). See [[../topics/cherry-pruning|Cherry pruning]].

**Other timing nuances**:
- Brambles: summer tipping (June-July) + post-harvest cleanup + late-winter thinning. Three operations per year.
- Roses: late winter (forsythia bloom).
- Blueberry: late winter dormant; rabbiteye allows late-July post-harvest as alternative.
