# Pruning Sanitation

Pruning shears are vector. Diseases that move with sap or spores travel on tools from one plant to the next. The fix is cheap and fast: a 30-second dip in a disinfectant between bushes.

## Tool disinfectants

Two effective options:

### 70% isopropyl alcohol (preferred)
- Fast-acting (30 seconds)
- Doesn't corrode steel
- Doesn't need rinsing
- Buy 70% rubbing alcohol; pour into a tall narrow container (a tall yogurt cup or a sealed jar)
- Dip blade for ~30 seconds between bushes
- This is the **preferred** option for rose pruning (Knock Out RRD prevention) and any disease-prone work

### 10% bleach (1 part bleach to 9 parts water)
- Fast-acting (30 seconds)
- Cheaper than alcohol
- **Corrosive** — rinse and dry tools after the session
- Works fine in a pinch
- Mix fresh; bleach loses potency in solution after a few hours

> "Tools should be disinfected for at least 30 seconds in a 10% bleach solution (1 part bleach to 9 parts water) or, preferably, 70% alcohol."
> — UW-Madison (raspberry sanitation)

> "Dip your pruning shears periodically in a 70 percent alcohol solution."
> — Clemson HGIC 1173 (rose baseline)

### Don't bother with
- Lysol / household disinfectant sprays (some work, but ineffective in the field on sap-coated blades)
- Wiping with a paper towel (mechanical only — doesn't sanitize)

## When to disinfect

| Frequency | Situation |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Between every bush** | Rose pruning (Rose Rosette Disease prevention) |
| **Between every cherry tree** | Sweet cherry (bacterial canker, Cytospora) |
| **Between cuts within a diseased plant** | Cane blight, fire blight, RRD-suspect tissue |
| **End of session** | Always — wipe blades dry, light coat of oil for storage |
| **Optional** | Healthy berry bushes with no disease pressure |

## Plant-by-plant disease watch

### Sweet cherry (Kristin, BlackGold)
- **Bacterial canker (Pseudomonas syringae pv. syringae)** — orange-amber gummosis, sunken cankers, cracked bark. Pruning timing is the primary defense; tool sanitation is secondary.
- **Cytospora / Leucostoma canker** — fungal sister to bacterial canker; same prevention via late-July pruning.
- **Silver leaf (Chondrostereum purpureum)** — silvery sheen on leaves; spores enter through fresh dormant cuts. Summer pruning eliminates exposure.
- **Action**: cut **4-6 inches below visible canker** into clean wood; sterilize tools; bag and burn prunings. Do NOT compost.

### Roses (Knock Out)
- **Rose Rosette Disease (RRD)** — eriophyid mite-vectored virus. Symptoms: witches' broom, excessive thorns, red distorted leaves, abnormally thick canes. **Sap-transmissible on tools.**
- **Action**: If RRD confirmed, **uproot the entire bush** — pruning won't save it; the virus is systemic. Bag at the plant. Do not compost. **Sterilize tools** before touching another rose.
- **Black spot, powdery mildew** — fungal, not sap-transmitted, but dirty tools spread spores. Sanitize between bushes anyway.

### Brambles (raspberry, blackberry)
- **Anthracnose, cane blight, spur blight, orange rust** — all overwinter on old canes. Removing spent floricanes immediately after harvest is the primary defense.
- **Action**: prune 4-6 inches below visible disease symptoms. Sterilize tools 30s in 10% bleach or 70% alcohol. **Burn or bury** prunings (UW protocol).

### Blueberries
- **Mummy berry (Monilinia vaccinii-corymbosi)** — overwinters in fallen fruit and mulch, NOT pruning wounds. Sanitation focus is ground cleanup, not tool hygiene.
- **Phomopsis, Botryosphaeria cankers** — uncommon in well-pruned bushes; sanitize tools if seen.

## Disposal

| Material | Disposal |
|----------|---------|
| Healthy, disease-free prunings | Compost or chip for mulch |
| Spent bramble floricanes (no visible disease) | Bag for municipal yard waste, or burn |
| Any cane with cankers, gummosis, witches' broom, blight | **Burn or bag for trash. Never compost.** |
| RRD-suspect rose plant | Bag at the plant before moving. Trash, do not compost. |

Burning is best where allowed. Composting kills most fungal spores at proper hot-pile temps but doesn't reliably kill viruses (RRD, mosaic) or persistent bacterial cells. Don't risk it for diseased material.

## Storage and tool maintenance

- After each session, wipe blades clean.
- Apply a thin coat of light oil (3-in-1, sewing machine oil, or any food-safe mineral oil) to prevent rust.
- Sharpen as needed (see [[cut-mechanics|Cut mechanics]]).
- Store dry. A moldy garage shelf rots wooden handles and rusts blades.
