# Cane Renewal Pruning

Multi-stem shrubs (blueberries, shrub roses) maintain productivity through **cane age management**: each cane has a productive life cycle, and you replace the oldest fraction every year so the bush always has a mix of vigorous young canes plus mature productive canes.

## The age-mix model

A well-pruned mature shrub has canes from multiple year-classes:

```
Year 1 cane: 1-2 new canes per year (replacements)
Year 2-4 cane: peak fruit production (these are the workhorses)
Year 5-6 cane: still productive, but declining
Year 7+ cane: low yield, wasting space — REMOVE
```

By removing the oldest 1-3 canes each year and letting 1-2 new canes establish, the bush stays in steady-state with a continuous productive workforce.

## The 20% rule

The most common consensus number: **remove ~20% of canes per year** (UGA C715 for rabbiteye blueberry; one-third equivalent for shrub roses).

- For a 10-cane bush → remove 2 oldest canes per year (5-year cycle)
- For a 6-cane bush → remove 1-2 oldest canes per year
- For a 15-cane bush → remove 3 oldest canes per year

This implies a **5-year complete turnover cycle** — every cane in the bush has been replaced once every five years. Roses in the one-third regime turn over in 3 years.

## Identifying old canes

Visual cues that a cane is old enough to remove:

| Plant | Old-cane signals |
|-------|-----------------|
| **Highbush blueberry** | Bark is gray, peeling, shredded; trunk diameter >1" (UMD); flower buds are sparse and concentrated at twiggy tips |
| **Rabbiteye blueberry** | Bark gray and weathered; trunk diameter >1.5"; cane >6 ft tall and unproductive (UGA) |
| **Shrub rose** | Bark gray-brown, woody; few flowers from this cane last season; cane is leaning/leggy |

## Where to make the cut

Two valid approaches:

### Ground-level cut (UMN/UMD/Clemson highbush)
Cut the old cane flush to the ground. New replacement cane breaks from the crown.
- Pros: clean, simple
- Cons: no cane on the cut location next year; bush thinner that side temporarily

### Stub cut at 0-24" (UGA rabbiteye, Clemson southern highbush)
Cut the old cane at 0-24 inches above ground, leaving a stub. Latent buds on the stub break vigorous replacement canes within a year.
- Pros: faster recovery; replacement canes break from a higher point and reach productive height sooner
- Cons: stub is ugly for a year; replacement may need thinning

> "Remove one to three of the largest canes each winter at 0 to 24 in. from ground level."
> — UGA C715 (rabbiteye blueberry)

For shrub roses, ground-level cuts are standard.

## Avoiding mistakes

### Don't replace too many canes at once
Removing more than 25% of canes in one year:
- Triggers excessive water-sprout regrowth (vigorous, weakly-attached)
- Reduces fruit for 1-2 years
- Stresses the plant

If a bush is overgrown and needs major work, do it across **2-3 years** in stages.

### Don't replace too few canes
Removing 0-1 cane per year on a mature bush means old canes accumulate. Within 5 years the bush is dominated by 7-10 year old wood that flowers sparsely on twiggy tips.

The discipline is **annually, religiously, take out the oldest cane(s).** It's easy to skip a year. Don't.

### Distinguish renewal from rejuvenation
Renewal: incremental, every year, oldest 1-3 canes.
**Rejuvenation**: a one-time hard reset (cut entire bush to 12-18" or to ground). Used for:
- Roses every 2-3 years (Knock Out tolerates this well)
- Severely neglected blueberries (last resort; sacrifices 2 years of fruit)

If you've been doing renewal annually, you should never need rejuvenation.

## Side-by-side: blueberry vs rose

| Attribute | Highbush blueberry | Knock Out rose |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Cane life** | 5-7 productive years | 3-5 years |
| **Renewal rate** | 20% / yr (1-3 oldest) | 33% / yr (one-third oldest) |
| **Cycle length** | 5 years full turnover | 3 years full turnover |
| **Cut location** | Ground level OR 0-24" stub | Ground level |
| **Total cane target** | 6-10 mixed ages | 5-9 mixed ages |
| **Hard reset alternative** | Rare — last resort | Every 2-3 years to 12-18" |
