# Cut Mechanics

How to make a clean pruning cut that heals fast and doesn't invite disease.

## Cut placement

### On a shoot or cane (heading cut)

- **¼ inch above an outward-facing bud.**
- Cut **angled at ~45°, sloping away from the bud** — water runs off the cut, not into the bud.
- The bud is the new growth point; the angled cut sheds water and reduces dieback.

> "Make a cut ¼ inch above the bud and angled at the same angle as the bud."
> — Clemson HGIC 1173

Why outward-facing? New growth follows the direction the bud points. Outward-facing buds direct new shoots away from the canopy center, opening the bush for airflow and light.

### On a branch (thinning cut)

Cut **just outside the branch collar** — the slight swelling at the base where the branch joins the trunk. Don't flush-cut into the trunk; the collar contains the chemical defense barrier the tree uses to wall off the wound.

The Three-Cut Method for limbs heavier than ~1.5 inches:

1. **Undercut** 6-12 inches out from the trunk, halfway through the branch.
2. **Top cut** an inch farther out — the branch breaks and falls cleanly without tearing bark down the trunk.
3. **Final cut** at the branch collar.

### Stub cuts (sweet cherry exception)

For sweet cherry, when removing a large lateral, **leave a 6-10 inch stub** rather than cutting back to the branch collar. Cornell research found stubs become walled-off dead-ends that contain bacterial canker infections; flush cuts let infection spread directly into the trunk.

> "Pruning to remove large lateral branches was done leaving a six to ten inch (15 to 25 cm) stub."
> — Carroll et al., NY Fruit Quarterly 2010

The 6-10" stub may produce some renewal shoots in subsequent years; if so, train the strongest one as the new lateral.

## Tools by cut size

| Cut diameter | Tool |
|--------------|------|
| Up to ½" | Bypass hand pruners |
| ½ - 1" | Bypass loppers (long-handle pruners) |
| 1 - 3" | Pruning saw (folding or sheath) |
| 3"+ | Larger pruning saw or chainsaw (rare on home-garden plants) |

### Bypass vs anvil

- **Bypass pruners**: two blades pass each other like scissors. Clean cut. **Use these for living wood.**
- **Anvil pruners**: blade meets a flat surface. Crushes wood. Only OK for dead wood; bruises and damages live cane.

For roses, brambles, and small fruit, **always use bypass.**

### Sharpness

- A sharp blade compresses the wood, then cuts cleanly.
- A dull blade tears the wood, leaving a ragged surface that holds water and fungal spores.
- Hone after each session with a small file or sharpening stone (a few strokes on the bevel side only).

## Sealants

Mostly unnecessary on small cuts. Two situations where sealants are recommended:

1. **Rose canes thicker than a pencil** — Clemson recommends sealing the cut end with nail polish or wood glue to prevent cane borers. Optional in regions without significant borer pressure.
2. **Large oak / elm cuts during oak wilt or Dutch elm disease season** — irrelevant for the plants in this wiki, but worth knowing.

For sweet cherry: **do NOT apply copper or phosphite to pruning wounds.** Cornell's 2010 study found these provide no significant protection against bacterial canker (8.1 cm canker untreated vs. 8.8 cm with copper — not significantly different). The protection comes from **timing** (late July), not chemistry.

## Bud and pith health checks

### Pith color (especially roses)

When pruning, look at the cut end:

- **White, plump pith** — the cane is alive, healthy. Good cut.
- **Brown, withered pith** — the cane is dead from this point down. Keep cutting toward the base until you see white.
- **Dark ring with hollow center** — borer damage. Remove the entire cane.

> "The center of the canes should be white and plump, not brown and withered." — Clemson HGIC 1173

### Bud type (especially blueberries)

- **Vegetative bud**: slim, narrow, pointed.
- **Flower bud**: plump, rounded — produces a cluster of flowers.

For young blueberries (years 1-2), remove flower buds entirely so the plant invests in root and cane growth instead of fruit. For mature blueberries with crowded twiggy tips, prune the whole tip to thin flower-bud density.

## Cap on how much to remove

For trees and shrubs, **remove no more than 20-25% of the canopy in one season.** Exceeding this:

- Triggers excessive water-sprout regrowth (vigorous, unfruitful, weakly attached)
- Stresses the plant and reduces next year's crop
- For cherry, can dump enough vigor that summer cuts won't seal in time

Knock Out roses and primocane-fruiting raspberries are the exceptions — both tolerate cutting 100% to ground (mow-down or hard reset).
