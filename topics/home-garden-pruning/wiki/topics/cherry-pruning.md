# Cherry Pruning — Kristin and BlackGold

Both Kristin and BlackGold are *Prunus avium* sweet cherries bred at Cornell-Geneva for Eastern conditions. The single most important pruning rule for sweet cherry in the East is **prune in late July, after harvest** — not in the dormant season — to dramatically reduce bacterial canker (*Pseudomonas syringae* pv. *syringae*) and Cytospora canker infection of pruning wounds.

## When to prune

**Late July, after harvest, on a dry day.** Cornell field trials (Carroll et al. 2010) showed canker extension on inoculated stubs was 11.2 cm from March cuts, 7.2 cm from May cuts, and **only 2.1 cm from late-July cuts** — a roughly 5x reduction. Leucostoma (Cytospora) canker followed the same pattern: 45 stubs infected from March pruning vs. **0 from July pruning**.

> "The best time to prune sweet cherry in terms of limiting bacterial canker infection may be shortly after harvest in late July."
> — Carroll et al., *NY Fruit Quarterly* 2010

**Trade-off**: July cuts produce far fewer renewal shoots than April cuts (only 13 of 189 stubs in Geneva re-sprouted in July vs. 44 of 189 in March/April). So accept smaller cuts, do renewal pruning incrementally each year, and don't expect heavy regrowth from summer cuts.

**Cultivar-specific**: BlackGold has documented "considerable resistance to bacterial canker" (per Cummins/Cornell). Kristin has no published canker-resistance rating. **Pruning timing matters more for Kristin** — be especially disciplined about late-July timing on Kristin, somewhat more flexible on BlackGold.

**Don't prune in fall, winter, or rainy weather.** Spores travel in moisture. Copper or phosphite sprays at the cut do almost nothing (8.1 cm canker with no bactericide vs. 8.8 cm with copper — not significantly different).

## What to cut

Standard sweet-cherry pruning cuts in priority order:

1. **Dead, diseased, or broken** wood — anytime, year round
2. **Crossing or rubbing** branches
3. **Water sprouts** (vertical shoots on scaffolds) and **suckers** from below the graft
4. **Inward-facing or downward-bent** branches that won't bear good fruit
5. **Limbs producing too much shade** in the canopy interior
6. **Renewal cuts** to remove ~20% of the canopy (or oldest fruiting wood, on systems like Spanish Bush or KGB)

**Cap removed wood at 20% of total canopy per season.** Removing more shocks the tree and triggers excessive water-sprout regrowth.

## Stub cuts, not flush cuts

When removing a large limb (≥ 1 inch), leave a **6-10 inch stub** — do not flush-cut to the trunk. Cornell research found stubs become walled-off dead-ends that contain canker infections; flush cuts let infection march straight into the trunk/scaffold.

> "Pruning to remove large lateral branches was done leaving a six to ten inch (15 to 25 cm) stub."
> — Carroll et al. 2010

For small cuts (under pencil thickness), cut just outside the branch collar — 45° slope away from the bud, ¼ inch above an outward-facing bud. See [[../concepts/cut-mechanics|Cut mechanics]].

## Training systems

Sweet cherry's natural vigor and apical dominance force active training. For the user's home-orchard scale with Kristin (standard rootstock, 15-18 ft mature) and BlackGold (Mahaleb, half-standard), the practical options are:

| System | Best for | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| **Modified Central Leader** | Default home-orchard pick | Single dominant leader with 4-6 well-spaced scaffolds at wide angles. Familiar, simple. |
| **Kym Green Bush (KGB)** | Pedestrian / no-ladder operation | Top tree at 8 ft. Multiple temporary vertical leaders renewed via stub cuts to ~10". *Don't use on non-spur cultivars (Regina, Attika).* Works for Kristin and BlackGold. |
| **Spanish Bush** | Low + spread-out | Multiple permanent leaders, lateral fruiting wood renewed annually. Hedge sides annually, top at 8 ft. |
| **Open Center / Vase** | Wet climates needing airflow | 3-4 main scaffolds, no central leader. Better light penetration but less vigorous structure. |

For full descriptions see [[../reference/training-systems-cherry|Cherry training systems]].

## Pollination tie-in

Kristin is **self-incompatible** and shares an incompatibility group with Bing, Napoleon, Lambert, Ulster, Somerset, and Emperor Francis — so it needs a non-group pollenizer within ~100 ft. **BlackGold is self-fertile and a universal pollenizer** for mid-to-late-blooming sweet cherries. Planting them together solves Kristin's pollination problem.

This is relevant to pruning because removing too much of either tree's flowering wood reduces cross-pollination. Don't prune both in the same year if you can avoid it; alternate, or prune lightly.

## Year-by-year protocol

| Year | Action |
|------|--------|
| **At planting** | Head nursery whip to 30-36" if training central leader (per Vogel CL); for KGB, head to 18". Remove broken roots. Stake. |
| **Year 1 (June-July)** | Select 4-6 scaffolds at wide angles (use limb spreaders or toothpicks). Remove competing leader if doing modified CL. |
| **Year 2 (late July)** | First post-harvest summer prune — only the most necessary cuts (water sprouts, weak laterals). |
| **Year 3+** | Continue late-July pruning. Renewal cuts on 5-7 yr old fruiting wood (~20%/yr cap). Stub-cut large limbs at 6-10". |
| **Mature** | Maintain canopy height (≤ 8 ft for KGB, taller for CL). Remove ~20% canopy/yr. Watch for canker (orange-amber gummosis at cuts) and prune 4-6" below symptoms. |

## Sanitation

- Sterilize tools between trees: 70% isopropyl alcohol (preferred for cherry — bleach corrodes blades). 30+ second dwell time. See [[../concepts/sanitation|Sanitation]].
- Bag and burn (don't compost) any pruning showing gummosis, dead bark, or sunken cankers.
- If a canker is found mid-season: cut **4-6 inches below visible symptoms** into clean wood, sterilize tools, dispose of the prunings.
