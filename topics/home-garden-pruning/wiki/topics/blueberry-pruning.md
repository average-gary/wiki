# Blueberry Pruning

Blueberries are pruned on a **cane-renewal** model: the bush is a cluster of canes of varying ages, fruit is borne on 2-4 year old wood, canes peak in productivity around year 5, and old wood (>6 years) gets less productive. Each year you remove a small fraction of the oldest canes to keep the bush in a productive age mix.

## When to prune

**Late winter / early spring while dormant** — after the worst cold has passed but before bud break. In mild winters this is January-March; in colder zones, late February through March.

**Exception**: rabbiteye blueberries can also be pruned just after harvest (Clemson, NC State allow late-July rabbiteye pruning) for size control without sacrificing next year's crop, since flower buds set on summer growth.

## Years 1-2: establishment

Goal is **roots and structure, not fruit**.

> "Remove low, twiggy growth entirely and tip remaining shoots to remove all the flower buds." — UGA

- **Remove all flower buds** in years 1 and 2 — pluck off any flowers/fruit that form. This forces the plant to put energy into roots and a strong cane framework, doubling the productive life of the bush.
- Light pruning only: dead wood, crossing branches, low twiggy growth.
- UGA aggressive variant: remove **one-third to one-half of the plant at planting**.
- NC State even more aggressive: 2/3 cutback at planting.

## Years 3-5: light shaping, allow first crops

- Allow 1-2 new strong canes per year to come up from the crown.
- Remove weak twigs and low spreading branches.
- Year 3-4: thin flower buds by ~50% to keep crop load reasonable on a still-young bush (NC State rule: rub off half the buds with your thumb in late winter).
- Begin removing the lowest-producing twiggy growth at branch tips.

## Mature (year 6+): cane renewal cycle

This is the core of mature-blueberry pruning.

### Highbush (Northern and Southern)

**Annual cuts** (late winter):
1. Remove **dead, diseased, or broken** canes at ground level.
2. Identify the **2 oldest canes** — typically 6+ years, with peeling/shredded gray bark and trunks >1" diameter (UMD's age-by-bark rule).
3. **Cut those 2 oldest canes back either to a strong young lateral OR to within 1 ft of the ground.** This stub-cut allows latent buds to break a vigorous replacement cane (Clemson, UGA southern-highbush rule).
4. **Thin twiggy tip growth** that has too many crowded flower buds — concentrated buds → too many small berries on weak wood.
5. **Open the center** for light penetration.

**Cane-count target by region**:
- Cold zones (UMN): **4-6 older canes + 1-2 new canes per year** (smaller bush)
- Mid-Atlantic (UMD): **12-18 canes total** (larger, vigorous bushes)

The wide range reflects climate and cultivar vigor. **Aim for 6-10 canes of mixed ages (1-2 of each year-class) for a typical home-garden highbush.**

### Rabbiteye

Rabbiteye is more vigorous, taller (can hit 10+ ft), and pruned somewhat differently.

> "Remove one to three of the largest canes each winter at 0 to 24 in. from ground level **or a total of about 20 percent of the canopy**." — UGA C715

- **Don't begin renewal pruning until the bush is 4-6 ft tall** (UGA).
- **Renewal cycle ≈ 5 years** — one fifth of the canopy replaced each year, so the entire bush turns over every 5 years.
- **Cane target: 7 canes** in the active age mix (Clemson).
- **Cuts at 0-24 inches above ground** (NOT flush) — leaving a stub allows latent buds to break a strong replacement cane. This is the key rabbiteye nuance vs. northern highbush.
- **Height cap at 6 ft each winter** — top out tall canes so fruit stays in reach.

## Bud identification

Knowing your buds helps you control crop load:

- **Vegetative buds**: slim, narrow, pointed — these are leaves and shoot tips.
- **Flower buds**: plump, rounded — these become flower clusters with multiple flowers each.

In a young bush, rub off half the flower buds with your thumb to reduce crop load. In mature bushes with too many buds at twiggy tips, prune the whole tip back to thin them.

## How much to remove total

**Cap at ~25% of the bush per winter.** UGA and Clemson recommend 20%. NC State and UMD note that mature bushes can take more if neglected, but aggressive cuts trigger excessive vegetative regrowth and reduce fruit for 1-2 years.

## Sanitation

- Less critical than for stone fruit or roses. Mummy berry (the main concern) overwinters in fallen fruit/mulch, not pruning wounds.
- Still: clean tools between bushes if any cane shows blight or canker.
- Burn or bag any cane with sunken cankers or extensive dieback.

## Year-by-year protocol

| Year | Action |
|------|--------|
| **Year 1 (plant)** | Plant. Remove flower buds. Tip lightly. UGA/NC State variant: 1/3-2/3 cutback at planting for vigorous regrowth. |
| **Year 2** | Remove flower buds again. No fruit yet. |
| **Year 3** | Allow first crop. Thin half the flower buds (NC State). Remove weakest twigs. |
| **Year 4-5** | Light shaping. Allow 1-2 new canes per year. Begin removing oldest, weakest. |
| **Year 6+ (mature)** | Annual: remove 1-3 oldest canes (~20% canopy); thin twiggy tips; open center. |
| **Mature rabbiteye** | Same + height cap at 6 ft. |
