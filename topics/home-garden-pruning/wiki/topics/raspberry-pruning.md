# Raspberry Pruning

Raspberries follow the same primocane → floricane biennial cycle as blackberries (see [[../concepts/cane-and-cut-biology|Cane and cut biology]]), but the technique varies sharply by **species** (red/yellow vs black/purple) and **bearing habit** (summer-bearing vs fall-bearing). Identify your cultivar before you prune.

## Identify what you have

| Type | Examples | Bearing | Pruning style |
|------|---------|---------|---------------|
| **Red/yellow summer-bearing** | Latham, Boyne, Killarney, Anne (yellow) | One crop on floricanes, mid-summer | Selective — keep primocanes, remove spent floricanes |
| **Red/yellow fall-bearing** (primocane-fruiting) | Heritage, Caroline, Polana, Autumn Bliss | One fall crop on primocane tips (or summer + fall via double-crop) | **Mow-down** all canes annually — simplest option |
| **Black raspberry** | Jewel, Bristol, Mac Black | One crop on floricanes | Summer tip + remove spent floricanes |
| **Purple raspberry** | Royalty, Brandywine | One crop on floricanes | Like black, slightly more vigorous |

**If unsure**: red/yellow canes on a trellis with mid-summer fruit = summer-bearing red. Red/yellow canes that fruit in late August/September on the new year's growth = fall-bearing. Stationary "hills" with arching canes that don't sucker into the row = black or purple.

## Summer-bearing red/yellow raspberries

**Three pruning operations per year:**

### 1. Post-harvest (mid-late summer)
> "Cut all canes that have produced fruit to ground level and remove them." — UMN Extension

The floricanes are dying; cutting them out immediately removes disease reservoirs (cane blight, anthracnose, spur blight) and opens space and light for the new primocanes.

### 2. Late winter / early spring — thin primocanes
**Target: 4-5 sturdy canes per linear foot of row** (UMN/UMaine consensus; UMass and CSU say 4-6).

Remove:
- Weak, spindly canes (less than pencil thickness)
- Winter-damaged canes (brown pith)
- Canes that wandered more than 12 inches outside the row

In cold-injury-prone climates, **delay thinning until spring** when winter hardiness is apparent.

### 3. Spring — head canes
> "Before growth starts in spring, cut the canes to about 12 inches above the support."

If your trellis top wire is 4 ft, head canes to ~5 ft. Don't head back more than 25% of cane length — you'll reduce yield.

## Fall-bearing red/yellow raspberries

**Easy mode: mow-down**

> "Cut all canes off at the base before growth begins in spring. Fruit will be produced on primocanes in late summer or fall."

This is the simpler default and is recommended by UMN, CSU, and most extension services for clean fall crops, easier disease control, and no winter-protection requirements (no canes to lay down in cold zones).

**Hard mode: double-crop**

If you want both a smaller summer crop and a smaller fall crop from the same plants:

1. Don't mow down. Treat as summer-bearing.
2. Thin primocanes to 4-5/ft.
3. The primocanes that fruited in fall will fruit again in summer of year 2 from the lower portions of the cane.
4. After summer harvest, cut just the spent fruited portion back to the last visible node (where fruit was). The cane keeps living.
5. Then cut to ground after the regrowth fall crop the following year.

**Trade-off**: double-cropping cuts your fall yield in half and increases disease pressure. Mow-down is recommended unless you specifically want a small summer crop.

## Black and purple raspberries

These don't sucker into rows — they form stationary "hills" or crowns. Plant 4 ft apart.

### Summer tipping (June, when primocanes are tall enough)
> "When primocanes are between 24 to 30 inches in height, pinch out the tip of each shoot to induce branching." — UMN

Black raspberries: tip at **24-30 inches**.
Purple raspberries: tip slightly higher, **30-36 inches** (more vigorous), though some sources (UMN) group them at 24-30".

This forces lateral branches — the laterals are where next year's fruit forms.

### Post-harvest (after the summer crop)
**Cut all spent floricanes to ground level immediately.**

### Late winter / early spring
1. **Select 4-5 strongest canes per hill** (UMN); UMaine and UMass go up to 5-7.
2. **Shorten side branches (laterals) to 12-18 inches** long.

That's the whole protocol for blacks/purples.

## Trellising

Reds need support — canes are tall and floppy when fruit-laden. Common setups:

- **Single wire** at 3 ft (CSU minimal)
- **Two wires**: 3 ft and 4-5 ft (most home gardens)
- **Three wires**: 2, 3, 4 ft (UMN — best support for tall reds)

Black and purple raspberries don't need a trellis if summer-tipped properly — the laterals are short and self-supporting.

## Sanitation

UW-Madison protocol for cane diseases (anthracnose, spur blight, cane blight):

- **Disinfect tools 30 seconds in 10% bleach** OR (preferred) **70% alcohol** between bushes.
- **Prune 4-6 inches below visible disease symptoms** into clean wood.
- **Burn or bury diseased prunings.** Do not compost.
- Plant only "disease-free plants from a reputable nursery."
- Destroy wild brambles within ~600 ft.

See [[../concepts/sanitation|Sanitation]].

## Year-by-year protocol summary (summer-bearing red, the most common type)

| Time | Action |
|------|--------|
| Late winter / early spring | Thin primocanes to 4-5/ft; head to 12" above support; tie to trellis |
| Late spring | Train new primocanes within 12" of row |
| Mid-summer (after harvest) | Cut spent floricanes to ground; remove from site |
| Late summer | Tie new primocanes loosely to trellis as they reach support height |
| Fall | Inspect for disease; light cleanup |
