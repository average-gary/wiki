# Shrub and Knock Out Rose Pruning

Knock Out roses (and similar shrub roses — Drift, Oso Easy, Easy Elegance) are bred for low-maintenance landscape use. They tolerate, but do not require, the aggressive every-spring pruning that hybrid teas need. The two essential moves: **a hard reset every 2-3 years** plus **annual one-third renewal**.

## When to prune

**Late winter / early spring, just as buds begin to swell** — but before leaf-out. Phenological cue: when forsythia is in bloom locally. Calendar windows by climate:

- **Mid-Atlantic / Coastal South**: mid-February to early March
- **Upper South / Mid-Atlantic interior**: late February to mid-March
- **Mountains / colder zones**: late March to mid-April

> "Prune hard ... in the early spring just as the buds begin to swell." — Clemson HGIC 1173

A separate **light cane-shortening to ~3 ft in late fall** is wind-protection only (prevents winter whip-damage and rocking) — it is NOT structural pruning. Save the structural work for spring.

## How much to cut

Two complementary regimes:

### Annual: one-third renewal (the gentle approach)

Each year, remove **the oldest one-third of canes at ground level**. Leave the other two-thirds. This is a continuous renewal cycle that keeps the bush full of vigorous young canes without the shock of a hard reset.

> Clemson one-third / renewal pruning rule: take out the oldest third each year, leaving 2-3 cohorts of canes at any time.

### Every 2-3 years: hard reset

Cut the entire bush down to **12-18 inches** from ground level. Knock Outs tolerate this aggressively — they bounce back the same season with a flush of strong young canes, and bloom by early-to-mid summer. Use the hard reset to:

- Control size when the bush has gotten leggy
- Reset shape after a winter with significant cane dieback
- Remove old, woody, low-flowering canes in one operation

In between hard resets, do annual maintenance (next section).

## Annual maintenance pruning (off-reset years)

1. **Remove dead, broken, or diseased wood** — back to clean white pith. Brown pith means the cane is dead from that point down; keep cutting.
2. **Remove crossing or rubbing canes** to open the center for airflow (black spot prevention).
3. **Remove inward-growing canes** that are crowding the center.
4. **Cut to outward-facing buds** so new growth angles outward, not inward.
5. **Light shape-up** — shorten remaining canes by ~1/3 to balance the silhouette.
6. **Light shearing mid-season** is optional and can encourage a stronger second flush; Knock Outs are technically self-cleaning so deadheading isn't required, but a mid-summer haircut keeps the bush tidier and pushes more flowers.

## Cut mechanics

- Make the cut **¼ inch above an outward-facing bud**.
- **45° slope away from the bud** so water sheds away (prevents bud rot).
- Cane-health check: **white pith inside is healthy, brown pith is dead** — keep cutting down until you see white.
- Optional: seal cuts on canes thicker than a pencil with a dab of nail polish or Elmer's wood glue to deter cane borers (regional concern; skip if borers aren't a problem).

## Sanitation: this matters more than you'd think

**Rose Rosette Disease (RRD)** is a virus spread by an eriophyid mite. It is moving rapidly through Knock Out plantings across the US Mid-Atlantic and Southeast. **Pruning sanitation is a primary defense vector** because RRD is sap-transmissible on tools.

- **Sterilize pruners between every bush** — 70% isopropyl alcohol or a 1:9 bleach dip (10% bleach), 30+ seconds dwell.
- **If you suspect RRD** (witches' broom growth, excessive thorns, red distorted leaves, or extra-thick canes): the entire plant must be **uprooted and bagged at the plant** — do not just prune out the affected canes. The pathogen is systemic.
- **Do not compost** suspect material. Bag and trash, or burn.
- For all rose pruning regardless of RRD pressure, the alcohol-dip habit is the baseline.

> "All pruning cuts on canes greater than the thickness of a pencil should be sealed with nail polish or wood glue to prevent cane borers." — Clemson HGIC 1173 (optional)

## Knock Out vs hybrid tea — why the protocol differs

- Hybrid teas are pruned hard (down to 15-18", 3-5 canes only) every spring because they bloom on new wood and benefit from forced rejuvenation.
- Knock Outs **also** bloom on new wood but are vigorous enough to push plenty of new wood from a much taller framework. The 2-3 year hard-reset cycle achieves the same renewal benefit without annual stress.
- Don't apply hybrid-tea protocol to Knock Outs every year — you'll dwarf them and reduce bloom mass.

## Year-by-year protocol

| Year | Action |
|------|--------|
| **Year 1 (planting)** | Light pruning only — remove dead/damaged. Let bush establish. |
| **Year 2** | Annual maintenance: dead/crossing/inward; one-third renewal. |
| **Year 3** | Hard reset OR continue one-third renewal. |
| **Year 4-5** | Annual maintenance + one-third renewal. |
| **Year 6 (or whenever leggy)** | Hard reset — cut all canes to 12-18". |
