# Thornless Blackberry Pruning

Blackberries are biennial-caned: a **primocane** grows vegetatively in year 1, becomes a **floricane** in year 2, fruits, then dies. Pruning is built around that two-year cycle plus the plant's growth habit (erect / semi-erect / trailing).

## Identify your cultivar's habit

Thornless cultivars split into three habits, each pruned differently:

| Habit | Cultivars | Trellis needed? | Summer tip height |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Erect** | Apache, Natchez, Ouachita, Navaho | Optional | 30-42 in (UGA: 40-42; Clemson: 30-36) |
| **Semi-erect** | Triple Crown, Black Satin, Chester (sometimes called trailing) | Yes — V or T trellis | 4 ft (~48 in) |
| **Trailing** | Some Chester forms, older cultivars | Yes — 2-wire trellis | 6 in above top wire |

The user's "thornless blackberries" are most likely Triple Crown, Chester, or Apache/Navaho. The protocol below works for any of them; note the table differences.

## The two-year cycle

```
Year 1:  Primocane emerges → grows tall → SUMMER TIP it → laterals form → goes dormant
Year 2:  Same cane is now a Floricane → flowers → fruits → senesces → CUT TO GROUND immediately
         Meanwhile new primocanes are emerging beside it (year 1 of next cohort)
```

This means **at any time you have a mix of canes** — current-year primocanes (vegetative) and current-year floricanes (fruiting then dying). The floricanes have brown/woody bark; primocanes are green/fleshy.

## Summer pruning (June-July)

**Tip primocanes when they reach the cultivar's target height** (see table above). This forces lateral branching, which is where next year's fruit comes from.

> "Cut...to a height of 40 to 42 inches in early summer to encourage lateral shoot development." — UGA C766 (erect)

**Tip with thumb-and-fingernail or sharp pruners**, removing the top 2-4 inches of the cane.

For trailing types on a trellis, instead tip canes about **6 inches above the top wire** to keep them at training height.

## Post-harvest (late summer)

**Remove all spent floricanes immediately at ground level** as soon as the last berries are picked. Floricanes are dead anyway; leaving them in place harbors anthracnose, cane blight, orange rust, and overwintering insects.

> "After harvest, remove and destroy canes that have fruited or are weak."

Bag, burn, or send to municipal yard waste — **do not compost** spent canes if any disease was present.

## Dormant pruning (late winter, before bud break)

Two tasks:

1. **Thin primocanes (now this year's floricanes)** to 5-7 strong canes per crown / hill (UMaine), or to a tidy hedgerow density. Remove the spindly, broken, or winter-damaged ones.
2. **Shorten laterals** that formed from last summer's tipping. Consensus targets:
   - **~12 inches / 12 buds** (UMaine, UMN consensus)
   - or **one-third to one-half of lateral length** (UGA — adjusts for cultivar vigor)
   - Stark Bros gives 16-18" as the long end of the range

Goal: enough laterals to bear a full crop, but not so many that the canopy shades itself.

## Trellising (semi-erect and trailing)

The simplest home-garden trellis for Triple Crown / Chester:

- Two end posts, 6-8 ft tall above ground
- Two horizontal wires: bottom at ~2.5-3 ft, top at ~5-5.5 ft
- Tie/weave canes to wires as they grow

For more aggressive cane separation, a **V-trellis** keeps last year's floricanes on one side (so they're easy to identify and remove post-harvest) and trains this year's primocanes on the other.

UK / RHS style for trailing types: train fruiting canes one direction along the wires and current-year primocanes the opposite direction — total physical separation makes spent-floricane removal trivial.

## Primocane-fruiting (Prime-Ark series) exception

If the user has a **Prime-Ark Freedom / Traveler / 45** cultivar (the Arkansas primocane-fruiting series), the protocol is different:

- **Cut all canes to ground level in late winter** for a clean fall-only crop on new primocanes.
- OR keep floricanes for a smaller summer crop + smaller fall crop on new primocanes (double cropping; lower yield per crop, more disease pressure).

Most extension services recommend the mow-down for clean fall crops.

## Sanitation

- Sterilize pruners between bushes if disease pressure is present (70% alcohol or 10% bleach 30+ sec). See [[../concepts/sanitation|Sanitation]].
- Always remove and destroy spent floricanes promptly.
- Anthracnose, cane blight, orange rust, double blossom — all overwinter on old canes. Remove them.

## Year-by-year protocol

| Year | Spring | Summer | After Harvest | Late Winter |
|------|--------|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **Plant year** | Plant dormant whips at 3-4 ft spacing | Let primocanes establish; tie to trellis if needed | (No fruit) | Don't prune hard |
| **Year 2 (first crop)** | Tie floricanes to trellis | Tip primocanes when they reach target height | Cut spent floricanes to ground | Thin primocanes; shorten laterals |
| **Year 3+ (mature)** | Tie up; check ties | Tip primocanes; remove suckers >12" from row | Cut spent floricanes immediately | Thin to 5-7 strong canes; laterals to 12-18" |
