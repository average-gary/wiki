# Rose Rosette Disease (RRD)

Rose Rosette Disease is the most serious threat to Knock Out and other landscape shrub roses in the US. It's a virus (Rose rosette emaravirus) vectored by an eriophyid mite. There is **no cure** — affected plants must be removed.

## Symptoms

Look for any of these in spring/summer:

- **Witches' broom** — clusters of dwarfed shoots from a single point
- **Excessive thorniness** — canes covered in many more thorns than normal, often soft and red
- **Distorted, red leaves** — small, deformed, deep red, won't green up
- **Abnormally thick, fleshy canes**
- **Distorted flowers** with extra petals or no flowers at all
- Eventual decline and death (1-2 years from first symptoms)

If you see any of these, do not prune the plant — pruning spreads the virus on tools.

## Transmission

Two pathways:

1. **Eriophyid mite (Phyllocoptes fructiphilus)** — wind-blown, lands on rose, feeds on tender new growth, infects.
2. **Mechanical (sap on tools)** — pruning a confirmed RRD plant and then a healthy one without sterilizing transmits the virus.

Transmission via sap on tools is the reason **disinfection between every rose** is non-optional in areas with RRD pressure.

## Management

> Pathogen is systemic — uproot the whole bush, bag at the plant, do not compost; sterilize tools (alcohol or dilute bleach) between bushes; sap on shears is infectious.
> — Clemson Rose Diseases (HGIC 2106)

### If you suspect RRD on a bush
1. **Do not prune.** Pruning won't remove the virus; it lives systemically in the plant.
2. **Uproot the entire bush** — root system included. Wilted/dead canes still harbor live virus.
3. **Bag the plant at its location** before moving it through the garden. Use a contractor bag or two layers.
4. **Trash, don't compost.** Don't burn near other roses (mites can ride smoke briefly).
5. **Sterilize tools** with 70% alcohol or 10% bleach (30+ second dwell) before touching another rose.
6. **Wait** — UMD recommends waiting 2-4 weeks before replanting in the same spot, but virus persistence in soil/dead roots is short.

### Prevention
- **Buy clean stock** — avoid no-name big-box bargains; purchase from nurseries that source from RRD-aware breeders.
- **Space roses ≥4 ft apart** — limits mite spread between plants.
- **Tool sanitation** as a routine baseline regardless of visible symptoms.
- **Inspect monthly** for early symptoms; remove early before mites spread to neighbors.
- **Avoid multiflora rose** (Rosa multiflora, the wild invasive) within sight — it's the primary RRD reservoir in the eastern US. If you have multiflora on your property, remove it.

### Mite control
- Foliar miticides labeled for eriophyid mites (e.g., bifenthrin) reduce mite populations but don't cure existing RRD.
- Hard pruning in spring may remove some overwintering mites, but not enough to be a stand-alone control.
- Tool sanitation is the highest-value defense for the home gardener.

## What RRD is NOT

- Not the same as **rosette virus** (a different virus on tobacco)
- Not the same as **black spot** (fungal — controllable with fungicides)
- Not the same as **powdery mildew** (fungal, white coating)
- Not "rose mosaic virus" (different virus, different symptoms)

If symptoms don't match the witches'-broom + thorniness + distorted-red-leaves combination, it's probably something else and the plant can be saved.

## Knock Out vulnerability

Knock Out roses are not specifically more susceptible to RRD than other roses, but their popularity and high-density plantings make outbreaks visible and fast-spreading. Early single-cultivar plantings (Knock Out red Radrazz hedges along highways and in commercial landscapes) showed dramatic RRD spread starting in the late 2000s. Mixed plantings and proper spacing reduce risk.
