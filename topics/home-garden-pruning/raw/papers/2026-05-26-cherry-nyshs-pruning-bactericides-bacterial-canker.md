# Evaluation of Pruning Techniques and Bactericides to Manage Bacterial Canker of Sweet Cherry

**Authors:** Juliet Carroll (NYS IPM), Terence Robinson (Horticultural Sciences), Thomas Burr (Plant Pathology), Stephen Hoying (Hudson Valley Lab), Kerik Cox (Plant Pathology) — all Cornell University, Geneva, NY.
**Published:** *New York Fruit Quarterly*, Vol. 18 No. 1, Spring 2010, pp. 9-16.

## Key findings

- **Late-July post-harvest pruning is the most disease-protective timing.** Canker extension on inoculated stubs in Geneva: 11.2 cm (March 25), 9.8 cm (April 24), 7.2 cm (May 22), and only **2.1 cm (July 28)** — a roughly 5x reduction. Highland NY trial showed the same pattern (14.5 cm March → 4.0 cm July).
- **Copper and phosphite sprays at pruning provide minimal protection.** Across April-pruned stubs in 2009, mean canker extension was 8.1 cm (no bactericide), 8.8 cm (copper), 9.0 cm (phosphite) — not significantly different.
- **Leaving a 6-10 inch pruning stub** when removing a limb may "distance" the trunk from PSS invasion. Stubs were as likely as flush cuts to become infected, but discoloration most often failed to progress into scaffolds or trunks because the stub becomes a walled-off dead end.
- **Leucostoma (Cytospora) canker** also followed the same pattern: 45 stubs infected from March pruning, **0 from July post-harvest pruning** — so summer pruning helps with both bacterial AND fungal canker.
- **Cautionary note**: in a Highland, NY commercial orchard pruned post-harvest in 2007, significant bacterial canker still developed and trees were lost — pruning timing alone is not bulletproof; "much we still need to understand about what triggers disease expression."
- Pruning timing was tested across training systems (Spanish Bush, Marchant, Perpendicular Vee) and rootstocks (MxM2, Gisela 5, Gisela 6); the timing effect dominated.

## Notable quotes / specifics

> "Field experiments on pruning date have provided evidence that the best time to prune sweet cherry in terms of limiting bacterial canker infection may be shortly after harvest in late July. However, this timing results in fewer renewal shoots from the pruning stubs." — abstract pull-quote, Carroll et al. 2010

> "We have found that copper or phosphite sprays applied to pruning wounds provide minimal to no protection against bacterial canker infection. The use of fungicides or bactericides to protect tree limbs and pruning cuts from cankers has rarely proved successful."

> "Key findings of our study include the potential for pruning stubs to contain bacterial canker infections in a dead end that is walled-off by the tree and the timing of late July, post harvest, as likely optimal for reducing colonization of tree limbs and trunks by Leucostoma and bacterial cankers."

> "Pruning to remove large lateral branches was done leaving a six to ten inch (15 to 25 cm) stub."

> "We found that cankers in the stubs pruned in April expand from July through September, from about fruit ripening to the onset of dormancy."

> Trade-off: April-pruned stubs produced lateral renewal shoots from ~1/3 to 1/2 of stubs; July-pruned stubs produced far fewer laterals (only 13 of 189 in Geneva July date, vs 44 of 189 in March/April). So summer pruning protects the tree but yields less regrowth — accept smaller cuts and renewal pruning if going summer-only.

## Source notes

Cornell University extension/research publication in NY State Horticultural Society's *New York Fruit Quarterly*. The five authors are senior Cornell extension and research professors in fruit IPM, horticulture, and plant pathology. The work was funded by NYS IPM Program Agricultural Grant and USDA Hatch Project NYG-625470. Field trials ran 2006, 2008, 2009 at Geneva NYSAES and Hudson Valley Laboratory. This is the most-cited primary research study underpinning current "prune sweet cherry in summer after harvest" recommendations across U.S. extension services. Highly credible and directly applicable to NY-grown Kristin and BlackGold cultivars (both Cornell/Geneva-bred). Cultivars tested in this study included Hedelfingen, Sweetheart, Lapins, Regina, Benton, and Attika — all *Prunus avium* — making findings broadly applicable to Kristin and BlackGold.
