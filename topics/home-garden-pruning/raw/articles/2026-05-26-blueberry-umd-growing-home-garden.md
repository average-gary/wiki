# Growing blueberries in the home garden (UMD Extension)

## Key findings

- **Timing:** Late winter / early spring (February-March in mid-Atlantic). Aimed at after worst-cold but before bud break.
- **Year 1 (planting):** Remove the top one-third to one-half of each main branch and strip *all* flower buds and open flowers. UMD is more aggressive at planting than UMN — they intentionally shorten the framework to push root growth and lateral branching, in addition to removing flowers.
- **Years 2-5:** "In February or March, remove weak, spindly shoots." Focus on removing weak shoots, crossing canes, and clearing center congestion to admit light. Light pruning otherwise.
- **Year 6+:** Begin renewal pruning. Remove oldest canes annually at the crown and retain the best new growth.
- **Mature target structure:** "12 to 18 canes of varying ages." This is **considerably higher than UMN's 4-6 canes** — reflecting a milder climate and bigger plants.
- **Annual replacement rate:** Plant naturally produces "3 to 5 new canes several feet long each year," which sets the ceiling on how many old canes can be removed without leaving the bush short of canes.
- **How to spot an old cane (visual cue):** "Old canes over 1 inch in diameter near the base naturally develop peeling, shredded bark, and stop producing fruit." This is gold for home gardeners — gives a tactile/visual marker rather than abstract "6+ years old."
- **Productive wood age:** "Fruits form on canes that are 2-8 years old with 5-year-old canes being the most productive." This is the most precise productivity-by-age figure across the sources reviewed. **Note:** other sources say fruit forms on 1-year-old wood (the laterals coming off canes); UMD is referring to the cane age, not the bearing-shoot age. Both are correct at different scales.
- **Berry-size sweet spot:** "Optimal berry size on moderately vigorous branches 12-18 inches long." Tip back overly long whips; don't over-tip moderate growth.

## Source notes

- UMD's cane-age framework (canes productive 2-8 years, peak at 5) is the most actionable scheduling rule of the sources reviewed: plan to retire any cane >6-7 years old.
- The peeling/shredded-bark visual cue is the most useful field-identification tip — beats counting cane ages.
- Cane-number targets diverge sharply by climate: UMN 4-6, Clemson rabbiteye 7, UMD 12-18, UGA-rabbiteye implies similar high counts. A home-garden compiler should note this regional spread rather than picking one.
- Year-1 head-back-by-half is more aggressive than UMN/UGA. The reasoning (root establishment) is consistent across sources but the magnitude differs.
- No rabbiteye coverage; mid-Atlantic guide is highbush-centric.
