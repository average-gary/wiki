# Pruning Cherry Trees: A Comprehensive Guide

**Author:** Alex Dover
**Publisher:** Raintree Nursery (Morton, WA — Pacific Northwest fruit-tree nursery aimed at home orchardists)
**Date published:** 2025-03-20

## Key findings

- **Default timing: dormant season**, "late winter to early spring, before new growth begins." But the regional caveat is critical for home growers in the PNW, NY, and other wet-spring climates.
- **Wet-region rule: prune late summer.** "In regions prone to wet winter and spring conditions, it's advisable to prune in late summer to minimize the chance of fungal infections." This aligns with the Cornell post-harvest research and is the most relevant rule for Eastern US, mid-Atlantic, and PNW home growers.
- **Ornamental flowering cherries: prune June into July** for the same disease-avoidance reason ("to prevent disease entry during dormancy").
- **Training system for backyard sweet cherry: central leader.** Maintain "a single main trunk with well-spaced lateral branches" — supports upright growth, harvest accessibility. Select 3-4 evenly spaced scaffolds with wide crotch angles; remove competing branches; keep canopy open for air and light.
- **Cut technique**: "at a 45-degree angle just above a bud or branch collar" and **prune during dry conditions** to minimize pathogen spread.
- **Disease awareness**: "improper cuts can impede healing and make the tree susceptible to fungal diseases like silver leaf."
- **20% rule**: "Never exceed 20% canopy removal in one season... over-pruning is a frequent error that can stress cherry trees and diminish fruit production."

## Notable quotes / specifics

> "In regions prone to wet winter and spring conditions, it's advisable to prune in late summer to minimize the chance of fungal infections."

> For ornamental cherries, "early to midsummer (June into July)" is recommended specifically "to prevent disease entry during dormancy."

> "Improper cuts can impede healing and make the tree susceptible to fungal diseases like silver leaf."

> "Never exceed 20% canopy removal in one season... over-pruning is a frequent error that can stress cherry trees and diminish fruit production."

## Source notes

Raintree Nursery is a long-established PNW home-orchard specialty nursery serving the wet-PNW climate — its guidance is shaped by direct silver leaf (*Chondrostereum purpureum*) and bacterial canker pressure in Western Washington. While not a peer-reviewed source (quality 3, not 5), this guide is one of the most direct, practical statements of the modern home-orchardist consensus: **default to dormant-season pruning, but switch to late-summer (after harvest) in wet climates and for ornamental cherries to dodge silver leaf and bacterial canker.** The 20% canopy cap and dry-day rule are practical, conservative, and widely echoed across extension publications. This is a useful counterpart to the Carroll et al. 2010 Cornell research because it translates the same principle into actionable backyard guidance and explicitly applies it to PNW conditions where Kristin and BlackGold may be grown.
