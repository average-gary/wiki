# Sweet Cherry Varieties for Eastern U.S.

**Author:** Robert L. "Bob" Andersen, Professor of Horticulture, Cornell University NYSAES, Geneva, NY (with Greg Lang of MSU and Jim Nugent of NW Michigan Hort Station as collaborators).
**Published:** Last revised 2004-01-27; presented at MI Orchard Show. Hosted on MSU Extension server.

## Key findings

- **Kristin pollination group**: Kristin falls in the same incompatibility group as **Napoleon, Ulster, Somerset, Bing, Lambert, and Emperor Francis** — meaning they will NOT cross-pollinate each other. Choosing a Kristin pollenizer requires a different group cultivar (BlackGold, BlackYork/NY 1725, Stardust, Hedelfingen, Gold, Regina, Sweetheart, Attika, etc.).
- **BlackGold context** (not in this paper directly, but contextualized by it): BlackGold is self-fertile and is recommended as a "universal pollenizer for any mid or late-blooming sweet cherry" — it can pollinate Kristin given bloom-time overlap. Andersen emphasizes self-fertile cultivars "will pollinate all other sweet cherry varieties that bloom at the same time."
- **Bacterial canker shapes Eastern variety choice.** Andersen calls Napoleon "just too susceptible to bacterial canker for EUSA," and rates BlackYork (a Kristin/Ulster-season pollenizer) as having "an excellent record for annual cropping without bacterial canker."
- **Climate hazards for sweet cherries in Eastern US**: "early bloom emergence and frost, winter cold hazards, especially to young trees, fruit cracking, and diseases like bacterial canker, brown rot, and cherry leaf spot all factor into site selection, variety & rootstock choices."
- **Kristin ripens mid-season** with Ulster ("BlackYorkT = NY 1725... ripens right with Ulster and Kristin"). NY 101 ripens ~10 days before Kristin/Ulster/Sam; NY 290 ripens ~8 days before; NY 8139 ~1 week before — useful for home orchard pollenizer planning.
- **All sweet cherry varieties crack** — even under rain covers. Site selection and canopy openness matter as much as cultivar.

## Notable quotes / specifics

> "Unfortunately EF [Emperor Francis] falls into the same pollination group as Napoleon, Ulster, Kristin, Somerset, Bing, and Lambert, so it is incompatible with these varieties."

> "All self-fertile varieties not only pollinate themselves they will pollinate all other sweet cherry varieties that bloom at the same time."

> On Eastern challenges: "Sweet cherries are near the top of the tree fruit crop list for challenges. Early bloom emergence and frost, winter cold hazards, especially to young trees, fruit cracking, and diseases like bacterial canker, brown rot, and cherry leaf spot all factor into site selection, variety & rootstock choices. A host of cultural imperatives must be learned."

> "BlackYorkT = NY 1725 was released by Geneva in 2003 ... This mid-season, dark cherry ripens right with Ulster and Kristin and has similar size and quality to them. Its big advantage that deserves your attention is that it will pollinate both of them as well as Emperor Francis."

> Andersen rates "BCT+ = bacterial canker tolerance is above average at Geneva" for BlackYork, Cavalier, Emperor Francis, Attika, Regina (but not for Kristin in this list — Kristin is referenced as a sibling of Ulster, with Ulster shown elsewhere as having decent canker resistance, but Kristin is not given a BCT rating in Andersen's "Best Bets" table).

## Source notes

Robert "Bob" Andersen was Cornell's lead sweet cherry breeder at Geneva NYSAES; Kristin (NYSAES release) and BlackGold (Cornell/Geneva, released 2000) are both products of the same breeding program he led. The cousin paper underlying Kristin's official cultivar description was published by NYSAES; Kristin itself is described elsewhere as "a mid-season, productive, large, black, high-quality sweet cherry" with above-average cold hardiness (originally bred for NY/Norway/Montana climate testing). This paper is most useful for **pollination planning** and **disease context** rather than direct pruning instructions, but it establishes that Kristin and BlackGold are NY-bred for Eastern conditions where bacterial canker pressure is high — strongly supporting the late-July post-harvest pruning protocol from Carroll et al. 2010 for these specific cultivars.
