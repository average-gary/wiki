# Rose Diseases — Rose Rosette Disease section (Clemson HGIC)

## Key findings

- **Symptoms to identify before pruning:** "reddened terminal growth on infected branches"; thicker, more succulent stems; "an abnormally high number of pliable thorns, which may be green or red"; smaller deformed leaves; witches'-broom-style excessive lateral branching; reduced flowering.
- **Vector:** the eriophyid rose-leaf-curl mite transmits the virus during feeding. Symptoms appear "within one to three months" of infection. Once the virus is established, "it moves throughout the plant, and the entire plant is infectious" — so partial pruning of affected canes does NOT cure the bush.
- **Removal protocol:** infected plants must be pulled (not just pruned) and then "burned or bagged for trash pickup." Also "remove any roots that might re-sprout later." Do **not** compost any RRD material.
- **Critical sequencing:** "Do not leave an uprooted infected plant in the garden, as the mites may leave this rose for other nearby plants." Bag immediately at the plant; don't drag across the bed.
- **Tool sterilization between bushes:** disinfect pruners with "rubbing alcohol or a dilute bleach solution" before working on the next plant — "sap on the pruners is contaminated with the virus." This is the reason routine 70% isopropyl or 10% bleach dipping is non-negotiable in RRD-active areas, even when working on apparently healthy Knock Outs.
- **Cultural prevention:** eliminate nearby wild multiflora roses (highly susceptible reservoir host); space plants so canes don't touch (limits mite walking); bifenthrin sprays every two weeks April–September on adjacent roses can reduce mite-mediated spread.

## Source notes

Clemson HGIC — university extension authority. Knock Out roses are heavily affected by RRD across much of the Southeast and Mid-Atlantic; this factsheet is the practical sanitation reference that pairs with any pruning guide. Key takeaway for the wiki: pruning a suspected RRD plant cannot save it — you remove the whole bush, bag at the plant, sterilize tools before touching anything else. The 10% household-bleach dip is interchangeable with 70% isopropyl per most extension services; Clemson lists rubbing alcohol or dilute bleach without specifying concentration. UMD specifies 1:9 bleach:water (10%) elsewhere.
