# Blackberry — Clemson HGIC Factsheet

## Key findings
- Blackberries follow a biennial cane cycle: primocanes (first-year vegetative) develop flower buds in late summer/early fall, then become floricanes (second-year) that flower, fruit, and die. Some modern primocane-fruiting cultivars produce on both cane types in a single year (two harvests).
- Summer (in-season) pruning of erect cultivars: when new shoots reach 30–36 inches, cut off the tips. Tipping forces lateral branching, controls height (target 3–4 ft), and increases fruiting sites for next season.
- Winter (dormant) pruning: shorten laterals on overwintered floricanes to 12–14 inches. This produces larger berries and easier picking. Combined with full removal of any remaining dead canes.
- Spent floricanes should be cut and removed promptly after harvest; this is sanitation as well as structural — it prevents disease carryover and opens the canopy for primocanes.
- Trellis options listed: V-trellis (angled T-posts, two wire heights at ~2 ft and ~5 ft) for airflow and light; hedge-row (I-trellis); standard T-trellis. V-trellis explicitly favored for airflow and sunlight access.
- Recommended thornless cultivars (mix of erect, semi-erect, and primocane-fruiting): Arapaho, Caddo, Chester, Hull, Natchez, Osage, Ouachita, Ponca, Prime-Ark Freedom, Prime-Ark Traveler, Sweetie Pie, Triple Crown, Von.

## Notable quotes / specifics
> "When the new shoots are 30 to 36 inches tall, cut off the tips to promote branching."

> "Prune the laterals to 12 to 14 inches for easier harvesting and larger berries."

> "Cut and remove all floricanes" after harvest, and continue tip-pruning primocanes through the season to shape developing canes.

> The V-trellis uses "angled T-posts with dual wire rows at 2- and 5-foot heights, maximizing airflow and sunlight."

## Source notes
Clemson Cooperative Extension — Home & Garden Information Center (HGIC) is a peer-reviewed extension publication from a southeastern land-grant university. South Carolina sits squarely in the climate range where most thornless blackberry cultivars (especially Arkansas-bred erect types and Triple Crown) perform well, so the cultural recommendations apply directly. Source is concise, home-garden oriented, and gives clean numeric targets (30–36 in tip height; 12–14 in lateral length) that match other extension guides. Limitations: no rotating cross-arm (RCA) trellis discussion; primocane-fruiting Prime-Ark winter-mow-down regime not explicitly covered.
