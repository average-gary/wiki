# Growing raspberries in the home garden (UMN Extension)

## Key findings

### Cane biology
- **Primocanes:** "green, fleshy stalks that grow each year"; develop brown bark and dormancy in year one before becoming floricanes.
- **Floricanes:** "woody and brown" second-year canes that produce fruit in early-to-mid summer, then die.

### Summer-bearing (floricane-fruiting) red & yellow raspberries
- **Post-harvest:** "Cut all canes that have produced fruit to ground level and remove them."
- **Cane thinning:** "Thin primocanes to 4 to 5 sturdy canes per foot of row."
- **Spring heading:** "Before growth starts in spring, cut the canes to about 12 inches above the support. Don't cut back more than 25% of each cane, to avoid reducing yield."
- **Cold-zone option:** In winter-injury-prone areas, delay thinning until spring when winter hardiness is apparent.

### Fall-bearing (primocane-fruiting) red & yellow raspberries
- **Single fall crop (mow-down):** "Cut all canes off at the base before growth begins in spring. Fruit will be produced on primocanes in late summer or fall." This is presented as the simpler, default approach.
- **Double-cropping:** Thin canes as in summer-bearing varieties. "The primocanes that produced the fall crop should not be removed, as they will produce fruit the following summer. Prune them back in spring to about 12 inches above the support, or to the last visible node that had fruit."

### Black & purple raspberries
- **Summer tipping:** "When primocanes are between 24 to 30 inches in height, pinch out the tip of each shoot to induce branching."
- **Post-harvest:** "After harvest, cut down all canes that bore fruit to ground level."
- **Spring lateral pruning:** "Cut back all side branches so they are 12 to 18 inches long."
- **Cane selection:** "Select 4 to 5 canes per hill, and prune out the rest."
- **Spacing:** Plant 4 ft apart; black/purple form stationary "hills" without root suckers.

### Trellis system
- "Sturdy support posts and several levels of wire or twine strung between the posts on either side of the plants."
- Wire spec: 12.5-gauge high-tensile wire with earth anchors, gripples, and strainers.
- Wire heights: "approximately 2, 3, and 4 feet above the ground."

### Row management
- Red/yellow: contain new canes "along the row or in between plants but not wider than 12 inches."

### Sanitation
- Plant only "disease-free plants from a reputable nursery."
- Destroy wild/abandoned brambles nearby.
- Remove weak/diseased plants from established patches.
- "After harvest, remove and destroy canes that have fruited or are weak."
- Apply copper to dormant canes if prior disease pressure exists.

## Source notes

- **Authoritative voice for upper Midwest** with cold-climate caveats.
- Distinctively, UMN frames the fall-bearing mow-down as the default and double-cropping as a secondary option — consistent with the "extension consensus often favors mowing for cleaner yields and better disease control" framing.
- Provides the cleanest, most concrete numeric protocol of the sources surveyed: 4–5 canes/ft for reds, 4–5 canes/hill for blacks/purples, tip primocanes at 24–30", winter laterals 12–18", trellis wires at 2/3/4 ft.
- Reviewed 2024.
