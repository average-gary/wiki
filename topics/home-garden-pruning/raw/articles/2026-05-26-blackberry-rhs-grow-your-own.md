# Blackberries — RHS Grow Your Own

## Key findings
- The RHS treats most blackberries and hybrid berries (e.g., tayberry, loganberry) as classic floricane-fruiting brambles — fruit borne on canes that grew the previous spring and summer. Primocane-fruiting cultivars (Prime-Ark Freedom etc.) are not the default in UK home culture, so the RHS guide does not lead with them.
- Annual pruning cycle is simple and structural: after harvesting, prune out the old fruited stems at the base. This stimulates new stems to grow the following spring for the next year's crop. This is the same "remove spent floricanes" principle other sources call out for sanitation.
- In-season cane management: loosely tie together the new (primocane) stems and aim to keep them in the centre of the plant, separated from the older fruiting stems trained out along the wires. This is the European "alternate-side training" approach: new canes one side / fruiting canes the other side, swapped each year. It's a practical alternative to the rotating cross-arm (RCA) trellis used commercially in the US for semi-erect thornless cultivars.
- Establishment year (first winter after planting): cut back any side shoots produced on the first year's stems to about 7.5 cm (3 in) long. This is a hard-pruning of the very first laterals to encourage strong-budded fruiting wood for year-2 harvest.
- Trellis: horizontal wires fixed to a wall, fence, or posts, spaced about 45 cm (18 in) apart. Lower wires hold the lowest tier of canes; higher wires hold the upper canes. This is a wall- or fence-trained system more common in the UK than in commercial US berry production.
- Spent-cane removal is described as essential: keeps these "vigorous and often thorny plants under control" and prevents the planting from becoming "a tangled, thorny mass."

## Notable quotes / specifics
> "Most blackberries and hybrid berries produce their fruit on stems (canes) that grew the previous spring and summer."

> "Prune out the old, fruited stems at the base, this stimulates new stems to grow in spring for a crop the following year."

## Source notes
The Royal Horticultural Society is the UK's leading horticultural authority; home-garden guides are written by RHS horticulturists rather than peer-reviewed extension faculty. Useful here for: (1) the alternate-side / centre-tie cane separation method, which is a legitimate alternative to T-trellis or RCA; (2) the first-winter establishment cut-back to 3 in side shoots, which most US extensions skip; (3) confirmation that floricane-only is the default model and primocane-fruiting cultivars are an exception. Limitations: no cultivar specificity for Triple Crown / Chester / Apache, no summer tipping height, no rotating cross-arm trellis discussion. UK climate and pest pressure differ from most of the US, so disease-sanitation timing may shift.
