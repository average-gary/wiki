# Growing Blackberries and Raspberries — UMaine Extension Bulletin 2066e

## Key findings
- Summer tipping of primocanes: pinch growing tips when new canes reach 4 feet. Tipping forces lateral (side-branch) formation; the next year's fruit is borne on those laterals when the cane becomes a floricane. Northern New England's 4 ft tipping target is taller than the 30–36 in cited by southern extensions, reflecting later season starts and slower spring flushes.
- Spring/dormant pruning has three coordinated steps: (1) remove all canes that fruited the previous year (spent floricanes), (2) thin the remaining primocanes-now-floricanes to 5–7 of the sturdiest canes per hill, (3) cut lateral branches back to roughly 12 buds (≈12 inches).
- The 12-bud / 12-inch lateral target after winter is consistent across multiple extensions and represents a practical balance between berry size (favored by harder pruning) and total yield (favored by leaving more fruiting positions).
- Tie remaining floricanes to support wires or posts to keep canes off the ground, improve sun exposure, and ease harvest. UMaine treats this trellis-tie as part of the spring pruning workflow rather than as separate trellis training.
- Sanitation is explicit and emphatic: remove all plant waste from the field after pruning and destroy it, preferably by burning or composting. Leaving dead canes in the planting encourages disease spread (anthracnose, cane blight, orange rust, Botrytis on fruit).
- The bulletin treats blackberry and raspberry pruning together but the cane-thinning and lateral-shortening targets apply directly to thornless erect blackberries.

## Notable quotes / specifics
> "Blackberries should have the growing tips of the new canes pinched off when they reach four feet. This encourages the canes to form side branches, or laterals, which will bear fruit in the following year."

> Post-harvest and spring: "Remove all canes that fruited after harvest. Thin remaining canes to 5–7 of the sturdiest per hill. Cut lateral branches to approximately 12 buds (about 12 inches). Tie canes to support wires or posts."

> "Remove all plant waste from the field after pruning and destroy it, preferably by burning or composting. Leaving dead canes in the planting will encourage the spread of diseases."

## Source notes
University of Maine Cooperative Extension publication 2066e (the bramble extension bulletin) is a peer-reviewed home-garden bulletin. Maine's climate puts canes under more cold and snow load than southern guides assume, so the 5–7 canes-per-hill thinning is relatively conservative and the burn-or-compost sanitation directive reflects long winters where Botrytis and cane diseases readily overwinter. The bulletin does not differentiate by cultivar (Triple Crown vs Chester vs Apache) but the underlying targets — 4 ft summer tip, 12 buds / 12 in winter laterals, 5–7 canes per hill — are widely portable.
