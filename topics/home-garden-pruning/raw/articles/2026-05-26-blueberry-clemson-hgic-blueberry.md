# Blueberry (Clemson HGIC)

## Key findings

- **Timing:** Dormant season, late winter most desirable. Mountain (cold) areas should wait for the worst cold to pass. **Rabbiteye specifically** can also be pruned immediately after harvest — Clemson is one of the few sources naming a post-harvest summer pruning window.
- **Years 1-5 (establishment):** Minimal pruning. Remove lower twiggy growth, dead shoots, weak spindly stems. Tip back excessively long shoots to force lateral branching. This is the framework-building phase.
- **Year 1-2 flower removal:** Remove all flower buds.
- **Year 3:** Allow some flower buds on vigorous shoots so the plant carries a small first crop.
- **Year 6+ (bearing plants):**
  - **Southern highbush:** Remove low spreading branches and those growing through the center, particularly weak/older ones. Cut back extremely vigorous 1-year-old shoots so they don't outcompete the rest of the canopy.
  - **Rabbiteye:** Up to 7 canes; remove the oldest or largest cane annually beginning in year 5. Renewal pruning becomes critical around year 6.
- **Renewal pruning protocol:** "Remove any weak or diseased canes entirely. Among the remaining canes starting with the older ones, cut back about two per year either to strong lateral branches or to within 1 foot of the ground." Cycle takes **4-5 years** to develop a new framework — i.e. you turn over the entire canopy roughly every half-decade.
- **Productive wood age:** Fruit on **1-year-old wood**, so vigorous annual extension growth is essential.
- **Highbush vs rabbiteye vigor:** "Highbush varieties reach peak production between 8-10 years of age and require more aggressive annual pruning than rabbiteyes to prevent overbearing." Rabbiteye is more vigorous overall and tolerates lighter touch in early years but needs decisive cane renewal once mature.

## Notable quotes / specifics

> "Remove any weak or diseased canes entirely. Among the remaining canes starting with the older ones, cut back about two per year either to strong lateral branches or to within 1 foot of the ground."

> "For mature rabbiteyes, maintain up to seven canes, removing the oldest or largest cane annually beginning in the fifth year."

> "Highbush varieties... require more aggressive annual pruning than rabbiteyes to prevent overbearing."

## Source notes

- One of the most complete single sources for **both rabbiteye and southern highbush** in one document — relevant because Clemson covers Carolina/southeast climates where both are grown side-by-side.
- The "cut back about two canes per year" rule is a practical heuristic that maps onto the ~20% renewal target many other sources cite (2 canes / 7-10 canes ≈ 20-30%).
- "Cut back to within 1 foot of the ground" is more aggressive than a flush ground-level cut — this leaves a stub from which a vigorous replacement shoot can break. Useful nuance.
- Rabbiteye-after-harvest pruning window is unusual and distinctive; most northern sources are dormant-only.
- Cross-reference UGA C715 for the southeast cane-number target (rabbiteye 4-6 ft height threshold + ~20% canopy renewal).
