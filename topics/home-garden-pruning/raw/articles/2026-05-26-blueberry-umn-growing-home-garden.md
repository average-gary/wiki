# Growing blueberries in the home garden (UMN Extension)

## Key findings

- **Timing (cold climate):** Prune annually in early spring before growth starts — "before new growth begins, after coldest weather has passed," typically March in Minnesota. Late timing avoids worst winter cold damage to fresh cuts.
- **At planting:** Only remove broken, dead, or dying parts of branches. No structural pruning year 1.
- **First two years:** Remove all flowers in spring so the plant invests energy in roots and canopy rather than fruit. UMN frames this as a non-negotiable for "healthy, productive plants for years to come."
- **Pruning objectives** (four explicit goals): remove dead and diseased wood; shape the bush; maintain enough vigorous main stems to prevent overbearing; stimulate new shoot growth.
- **Mature target structure:** "Four to six healthy older stems and one to two strong new shoots per mature bush." New shoots are explicitly framed as future replacements for the older stems — i.e. continuous renewal.
- **Productive wood age:** "Fruit is produced on one-year-old wood. The largest berries are produced on the healthiest wood, so a good supply of strong, one-year-old wood is desirable." This is the rationale for renewal pruning — old canes throw weaker laterals.
- **Renewal cuts:** Cut weak, old stems "that no longer produce strong young wood" off at ground level. Keeps the bush open and forces new basal shoots from the crown.
- **Caution against over-pruning:** "Take care not to prune too aggressively, as this can greatly reduce yield." Especially relevant for cold-climate gardeners worried about winter dieback who may already be losing tissue.
- **No summer pruning** mentioned — UMN treats blueberry pruning as a once-a-year dormant-season job.

## Notable quotes / specifics

> "Prune bushes before new growth begins, after coldest weather has passed."

> "During the first two years, remove flowers in the spring to encourage plant growth. This is necessary to ensure healthy, productive plants for years to come."

## Source notes

- UMN Extension; cold-climate (USDA zones 3-4) framing. Cane targets (4-6 older + 1-2 new) are notably lower than warmer-climate sources like UMD (12-18 canes), reflecting harsher winter conditions and typically smaller bushes that don't push as many basal shoots.
- The "one-year-old wood" framing aligns with the underlying biology (flower buds form on the previous season's growth), but oversimplifies — productive wood is generally 2-4 year old canes carrying vigorous one-year-old laterals. Cross-reference with UMD (5-year-old canes most productive) and Clemson (1-year-old wood) for the broader picture.
- Best as a Minnesota / northern-tier-state baseline. For mature-bush percentage-of-canes-removed targets, supplement with UGA / UMD.
- No coverage of rabbiteye, southern highbush, summer pruning, or bud identification.
