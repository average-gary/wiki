# CSU Fact Sheet 7.001: Raspberries for the Home Garden

## Key findings

### Publication
- Published April 2020, reviewed August 2025.
- Colorado State University Extension.

### Recommended varieties (Colorado-specific)
- **Summer-bearing (Front Range):** Boyne, Nova.
- **Fall-bearing (Front Range):** Jaclyn, Polana, Caroline, Heritage.
- **High elevation:** Plainsman (fall-bearing).

### Summer-bearing pruning
- "Remove the spent floricanes by cutting them at the ground after they bear fruit."
- Remove dead wood in spring.

### Fall-bearing pruning — two methods
1. **Mow-down (recommended as simplest):** "Mow or cut all the canes to ground level after the fall harvest and before spring growth." Eliminates summer crop but maximizes simplicity and disease control.
2. **Two-harvest:** "Remove only the portions [of canes] that fruited the previous year, allowing both summer and fall crops but with reduced fall productivity."

### Cane density
- Dormant-season target: "**five to six of the strongest canes per linear foot**" — at the higher end of the consensus range.

### Hedgerow geometry
- Width: "**2 feet wide at ground level**" — full hedgerow width including spread.

### Trellising
- Wires "typically positioned **3 feet above ground**" to contain and support canes (single-wire system common for small home plantings).

### After-planting heading
- Initial cane tops cut to **4–6 inches** after planting.

### Yield expectation
- A 25-foot row should produce "15 to 20 pounds of fruit per year under optimum conditions" by year three.

### Winter protection (Colorado-specific)
- Most summer-bearing varieties (except Nova and Boyne) need protection: lay canes down after Nov 1, cover with soil until April.
- Fall-bearing varieties avoid this since canes are mowed off post-harvest anyway — a meaningful argument for fall-bearing in cold/dry continental climates.

## Notable quotes / specifics

> "Mow or cut all the canes to ground level after the fall harvest and before spring growth."

> "Five to six of the strongest canes per linear foot."

> "Remove the spent floricanes by cutting them at the ground after they bear fruit."

> Yield: "15 to 20 pounds of fruit per year under optimum conditions" from a 25-ft row.

## Source notes

- **Best source for semi-arid / continental climate growers** (Front Range, mountain valleys, high plains).
- Distinctive practical insight: fall-bearing types skip the lay-down-and-bury winter protection because they're mowed each year — a labor-saving argument for primocane-fruiting in harsh-winter regions.
- 5–6 canes/linear foot is at the top of the consensus range; consistent with UMass's 4–6 upper bound.
- Lighter on black/purple raspberry detail than UMN — focused mostly on red raspberries appropriate to Colorado.
- 25-ft row → 15–20 lb yield is one of the few extension sources giving a concrete production benchmark for home gardeners to validate management.
- Reviewed 2025; current.
