# Pruning Shrubs — Renewal & One-Third Rule (Clemson HGIC)

## Key findings

- **Timing:** "Prune before spring growth begins." Pruning in late fall or midwinter exposes fresh cuts to cold damage; pruning at bud-swell minimizes that risk and lets the plant pour energy into directed growth. This timing aligns with the forsythia-bloom rule of thumb used in the Mid-Atlantic and Midwest.
- **One-third rule (multi-year renewal):** "At the first pruning, remove one-third of the old, mature stems. The following year, take out one-half of the remaining old stems..." continuing in year three until the bush has been entirely renewed without ever removing more than ~half of the canes in any single season. This is the gentle alternative to the 12–18-inch hard cut that some Knock Out guides recommend every 2–3 years.
- **Why this matters for Knock Outs:** Knock Outs do tolerate hard pruning to 12–18 inches every 2–3 years, but a steady one-third renewal cycle keeps the bush continuously presentable, avoids the bare-cane look in May, and is friendlier to gardeners who don't want one drastic year out of three.
- **Cutting technique:** when heading back shoots, "make the cut on a slight slant one-quarter inch above a healthy bud. The bud should be facing the direction preferred for new growth." For an open-centered shrub rose: pick an outward-facing bud.
- **Thinning vs heading:** thinning is "usually preferred over heading back" because it encourages interior growth and produces fuller plants rather than dense outer canopies with sparse centers — a real concern with mature Knock Outs that develop a hollow center if always sheared rather than thinned.
- **Cane diameter signal for renewal:** "old, mature stems" are the targets; identify the thickest, woodiest, least-productive canes at ground level and remove those first. New bloom comes from younger wood.
