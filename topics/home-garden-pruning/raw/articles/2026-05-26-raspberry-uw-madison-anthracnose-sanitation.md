# UW-Madison Extension: Raspberry Anthracnose & Sanitation Pruning

## Key findings

### Tool sanitation protocol (uniquely specific)
- "Decontaminate pruning tools between cuts by treating them for at least **30 seconds with 10% bleach** or preferably (because of its less corrosive properties) **70% alcohol**."
- The "preferred 70% alcohol" recommendation reflects the fact that bleach corrodes pruner steel; alcohol is gentler on tools.

### Disease-pruning margin
- "Prune **four to six inches below** where symptoms have appeared." This margin (well into apparently healthy tissue) is more aggressive than what general pruning guides suggest, because fungal mycelium spreads ahead of visible lesions.

### Disposal of diseased prunings
- Diseased canes should be **burned** where local regulations permit, or **buried** to prevent spore spread.
- Standard practice in extension literature: do NOT compost diseased bramble prunings — composting often does not reach sustained temperatures sufficient to kill cane-blight, spur-blight, or anthracnose pathogens.

### Preventative pruning role
- Even in the absence of symptoms, "regularly pruning established patches to remove diseased and winter-injured canes as well as to promote **better air flow**" reduces fungal pressure.
- Air flow is the operative mechanism — open-canopy thinning lowers leaf-wetness duration and inoculum load.

### Limitation of pruning alone
- "Pruning symptomatic canes may provide some benefit, but only when disease levels are relatively low."
- Once anthracnose is established, fungicides outperform pruning. This is an honest extension caveat — pruning is necessary but not sufficient for serious disease pressure.

## Source notes

- **Niche but uniquely valuable** — fills the sanitation gap left by the broader UMN/UMaine/UMass/CSU sources, which mention "remove and destroy" without specifying tool-decon procedure or pruning-margin distance.
- Anthracnose is one of the three classic raspberry cane diseases (with cane blight and spur blight); guidance generalizes to the others since they're all fungal cane pathogens.
- The 10% bleach / 30-second contact protocol is the standard horticultural surface-disinfection protocol — applies broadly across pruning of fruit trees, roses, etc.
- "70% alcohol preferred for tool longevity" is a practical detail rare in extension literature.
- Slightly weaker than the four primary sources for general pruning protocol but essential as the disease-management complement.
