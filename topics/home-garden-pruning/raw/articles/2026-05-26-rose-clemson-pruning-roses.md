# Pruning Roses (Clemson HGIC 1173)

## Key findings

- **Timing for repeat-blooming roses (hybrid teas, floribundas, and by extension most shrub roses including Knock Out):** prune hard "in the early spring just as the buds begin to swell." In the Carolinas this ranges from mid-to-late February near the coast to mid-April in the mountains — i.e. zone-dependent late-winter / very-early-spring window, before leaf-out.
- **Hard-pruning severity (hybrid tea / floribunda baseline):** keep only three to five of the healthiest, most vigorous canes; cut these down to 15–18 inches from ground level. Shrub roses tolerate this same hard cut but generally need it less often.
- **Cane health check:** "The center of the canes should be white and plump, not brown and withered." Brown pith = cane is dead or winter-damaged and should be cut back further until white pith appears.
- **Cut angle and placement:** make the cut 1/4 inch above the bud, "angled at the same angle as the bud" (functionally a ~45° slope away from the bud so water sheds away). Choose an outward-facing bud to direct new growth outward and keep the center open.
- **Open-center principle:** remove crossing canes to improve airflow — directly relevant to black-spot management on shrub roses including Knock Outs in humid climates.
- **Tool sanitation:** "Dip your pruning shears periodically in a 70 percent alcohol solution." This is the same protocol invoked for rose-rosette-disease (RRD) prevention, but Clemson cites it as a routine baseline regardless of disease pressure.
- **Cane sealant:** "All pruning cuts on canes greater than the thickness of a pencil should be sealed with nail polish or wood glue to prevent cane borers." (Optional in low-borer regions; widely debated.)
- **Deadheading:** remove spent blooms by cutting "a diagonal cut just above the next five or seven-leaf branch down on the stem" — encourages reblooming on classic repeat-bloomers. This contrasts with self-cleaning Knock Outs, which technically don't need it.

## Notable quotes / specifics

> "Prune hard ... in the early spring just as the buds begin to swell."

> "Make a cut ¼ inch above the bud and angled at the same angle as the bud."

> "Cut out all but three to five of the healthiest, most vigorous canes. Prune these canes down to 15 to 18 inches from ground level."

> "Dip your pruning shears periodically in a 70 percent alcohol solution."

## Source notes

Clemson HGIC factsheet — extension-authored, regularly updated, peer-reviewed by university horticulturists. This is one of the most-cited general rose-pruning references in the Southeast US. It does not call out Knock Out roses specifically (those go in the Growing Roses / HGIC 1172 factsheet) and does not address rose rosette disease (covered in HGIC 2106 Rose Diseases). For shrub-rose-specific severity, this factsheet's hybrid-tea protocol is more aggressive than what most extension services recommend for Knock Outs — pair with a shrub-specific source.
