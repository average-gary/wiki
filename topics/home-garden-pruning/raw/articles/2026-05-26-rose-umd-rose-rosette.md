# Rose Rosette Disease (University of Maryland Extension)

## Key findings

- **Early symptoms a pruner should recognize:** "reddening of the stems and stunted growth" — the first signs typically caught in late spring / early summer when comparing affected canes to normal new growth on the same bush.
- **Progression:** stem clustering with excessive soft thorns, flower abortion, witches'-broom appearance. The disease "can be fatal within two years," so timely identification at pruning saves the rest of a bed.
- **Why pruning alone fails:** the pathogen is systemic and located throughout the plant including the roots. UMD is explicit: "Infected roses should be uprooted and removed promptly." Cutting out a single symptomatic cane will NOT save the plant — the rest is already infected.
- **Vector and spread:** "transmitted by an eriophyid mite and by grafting." Wind carries mites between plants, which is why the protocol of removing nearby wild multiflora roses is recommended.
- **Sanitation rationale:** "Early detection is critical to prevent further disease spread within a planting." Surrounding roses must be monitored after a removal — the mites may have already moved.

## Source notes

University of Maryland Extension — region-specific authority for the Mid-Atlantic where RRD pressure is high on Knock Out and similar landscape shrub roses. This source corroborates Clemson's removal-not-pruning stance and reinforces that a suspicious bush identified during a routine late-winter prune should be flagged for full removal rather than partial cane removal. UMD does not specify bleach concentrations for tool sterilization in this resource; pair with the Clemson rose-diseases source or a tool-care reference for that detail.
