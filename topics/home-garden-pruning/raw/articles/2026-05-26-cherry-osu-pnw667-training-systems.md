# Cherry Training Systems (PNW 667)

**Authors:** Lynn Long, Gregory Lang, Stefano Musacchi, Matthew Whiting (OSU, MSU, WSU)
**Publisher:** Oregon State University Extension Service, Pacific Northwest Extension Publication PNW 667
**Date:** Published June 2015, reviewed 2023.

## Key findings

- Eight commercially viable sweet cherry training systems — but most apply to home orchards too. Choice depends on rootstock vigor, density goals, and whether grower wants pedestrian (no ladder) operation.
- **Kym Green Bush (KGB)** — fully pedestrian, simple and repeatable; multiple temporary vertical fruiting leaders renewed via stub cuts to ~10". Top tree at 8 ft. Not for non-spur cultivars (Regina, Attika). Works on full-size or semi-vigorous rootstocks.
- **Spanish Bush (SB)** — multiple permanent leaders, lateral fruiting wood renewed annually (stub back ~20% to keep spurs <5 yrs old). Hedge sides annually, top at 8 ft. Suits non-spur varieties.
- **Steep Leader** — 3-4 vertical leaders + horizontal scaffolds, pyramidal; treat each leader as one-sided spindle. Requires toothpicks at 3-4" shoot length to set wide angles.
- **Super Slender Axe (SSA)** — single leader, ultra-high density (10 ft rows × 20 in trees), 100% renewal of fruiting laterals annually via short-pruning. Demanding; needs dwarfing rootstock.
- **Tall Spindle Axe (TSA)** — central leader with continuous spiral whorl of moderate laterals, "Christmas tree" cone. Don't head at planting; develop 8-12 laterals first season; renew oldest 2-3 branches (5-6 yrs old) annually.
- **UFO (Upright Fruiting Offshoots)** — horizontal cordon trunk planted at 45° toward south with vertical leaders trained up wires; renew 15-20% of largest leaders annually (max 6-7 yrs old).
- **Vogel Central Leader (VCL)** — head nursery tree at 30-36"; minimal early pruning; renew ~20% fruiting wood annually. Requires dwarfing/semi-dwarfing rootstock to keep tree manageable.
- **General principle**: sweet cherry's natural vigor and apical dominance force active management — through rootstock choice, pruning technique (heading vs thinning cuts), and branch-angle manipulation.

## Notable quotes / specifics

> "Modern training systems must offer growers... precocity or early fruiting for high early yields... production on well-exposed wood of moderate vigor... a systematic process to renew fruiting wood... repeated canopy units for simplicity."

> KGB establishment: "Head nursery tree 18 inches above ground at planting. First dormant season: head all leaders to 2-5 inches. Second growing season: repeat heading prior to summer solstice." Mature: "top at 8 feet; remove lateral branches; renew leaders via stub cuts to ~10 inches."

> Spanish Bush vs KGB: "Vertical leaders remain permanent; laterals are renewed rather than entire leaders" — and "lateral branches are allowed to develop on each scaffold and fruit for several years before being renewed."

> VCL: "At planting: head nursery tree 30-36 inches; retain top two buds, remove next 5-6 buds." "Vigorous rootstocks produce very tall trees" — so home growers on standard rootstock for Kristin/BlackGold should NOT pick VCL with vigorous rootstock unless they have ladders.

## Source notes

Authored by four of the most-cited cherry-orchard scientists in North America (Long-OSU, Lang-MSU, Musacchi-WSU, Whiting-WSU). Peer-reviewed PNW extension publication, distributed through OSU/WSU/UI/MSU extension. Aimed at commercial orchards but the system descriptions and principles map directly to home-orchard training; the KGB and Vogel Central Leader systems are most often recommended for backyard sweet cherries on standard rootstock because they keep the tree pedestrian-accessible. For home growers planting Kristin (vigorous, standard, 15-18 ft × 15-18 ft mature) and BlackGold (Mahaleb half-standard), the most relevant systems are KGB (vigorous rootstock pedestrian), Spanish Bush, or modified central leader.
