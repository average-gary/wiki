# Seasonal Pruning Playbook — Home Garden

A calendar-keyed playbook for the user's five plant categories. Read top-to-bottom for the year, or jump to your current month.

The single most important rule across all plants: **timing matters more than technique.** Pruning a cherry in March will give it bacterial canker no matter how perfect your cut is. Pruning a Knock Out in October cooks it through winter no matter how clean your shears are. Get the timing right first.

## At-a-glance: when to prune what

| Plant | Primary window | Secondary operations |
|-------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Sweet cherry** (Kristin, BlackGold) | **Late July, after harvest** | Dead wood removal anytime |
| **Thornless blackberry** | Three windows: summer tip (June-July), post-harvest cleanup, late-winter thinning | — |
| **Shrub rose** (Knock Out) | **Late winter** (forsythia bloom — late Feb to mid-April by zone) | Light shearing mid-season for re-bloom |
| **Raspberry** (summer-bearing red) | Late winter thin + spring head + post-harvest cleanup | — |
| **Raspberry** (fall-bearing) | **Late winter mow-down** (cut all canes to ground) | — |
| **Raspberry** (black/purple) | Summer tip + post-harvest + late-winter laterals | — |
| **Blueberry** (highbush) | **Late winter while dormant** | — |
| **Blueberry** (rabbiteye) | Late winter primary | Late-July post-harvest as alternative |

## January — Late winter prep

**What to do**:
- Sharpen pruners, loppers, and saws.
- Top off your 70% isopropyl alcohol disinfectant container.
- Walk the garden, identify dead/winter-damaged wood across all plants.
- Watch the forsythia for budswell — that's your rose-pruning starting gun.

**Don't yet**:
- Don't prune cherry trees. Wait until late July.
- Don't prune roses until forsythia blooms.

## February — Late-winter pruning starts (zone-dependent)

### Roses (Knock Out)
**Timing**: as soon as buds begin to swell. In zone 7 mid-Atlantic this is mid-to-late February; further north, wait until March.

- **Annual maintenance** (most years): remove dead, broken, and crossing canes; remove inward-growing canes; cut to outward-facing buds; light shape-up of remaining canes by ~1/3.
- **Hard reset** (every 2-3 years): cut all canes to **12-18 inches** from ground level. Knock Outs tolerate this aggressively and rebound by early summer.
- **One-third renewal** (continuous): take out the oldest 1/3 of canes at ground level.
- **Sterilize tools between every bush** with 70% alcohol. Rose Rosette Disease is sap-transmissible. See [[../wiki/concepts/sanitation|Sanitation]].

### Blueberries (highbush and rabbiteye)
**Timing**: late winter while dormant; after worst cold has passed.

**Years 1-2**: remove all flower buds. Don't let the bush fruit. Light pruning only.
**Years 3-5**: light shaping. Allow 1-2 new canes per year. Year 3-4 thin flower buds by ~50%.
**Mature (year 6+)**:
- Remove **the 2 oldest canes** at ground level (look for gray, peeling bark and >1" trunk diameter)
- Or for rabbiteye, cut at **0-24" stub** to invigorate replacement growth
- Thin twiggy tip growth that has too many crowded flower buds
- Open the center for light
- Cap rabbiteye at 6 ft height

**Cane-count target**: 6-10 canes of mixed ages for typical home-garden highbush. See [[../wiki/topics/blueberry-pruning|Blueberry pruning]].

## March — Late-winter pruning continues + bramble work

### Raspberries (summer-bearing red, e.g. Latham, Boyne)
**Late winter / early spring before bud break**:
- **Thin primocanes** to **4-5 sturdy canes per linear foot** of row. Remove pencil-thin, broken, or winter-damaged canes.
- **Head canes** to ~12 inches above the trellis support. Don't head back more than 25% of cane length.
- **Tie to trellis** (12.5-gauge wire at 2, 3, 4 ft heights, or 3 ft and 4-5 ft).

### Raspberries (fall-bearing, e.g. Heritage, Caroline)
**Easy mode mow-down**:
- Cut **ALL canes to ground level** before growth starts. This is the recommended default.
- One clean fall crop in late August / September on new primocanes.

### Raspberries (black/purple, e.g. Jewel, Bristol)
**Late winter**:
- Select 4-5 strongest canes per hill; remove the rest.
- **Shorten side branches (laterals) to 12-18 inches** long.

### Blackberries (thornless: Triple Crown, Chester, Apache, Navaho)
**Late winter**:
- Thin primocanes (now this year's floricanes) to 5-7 strong canes per crown / hill.
- **Shorten laterals** that formed from last summer's tipping to **~12 inches / 12 buds** (or one-third to one-half of length per UGA).
- Tie to trellis.

### Roses (continued for cooler zones)
- See February instructions if you didn't prune yet.

### Cherries — STILL DO NOTHING
- Resist the temptation. March cuts on sweet cherry → bacterial canker. Wait until late July.
- Exception: emergency removal of broken or hazardous limbs only. Make stub cuts (6-10").

## April — Plant year care + spring buds

### Roses (mountain / cold zones)
- Late prune window if you missed earlier zones.

### All plants
- Watch for late frosts; nothing pruning-related to do.
- Mulch around bramble bases as they emerge.
- Check rose canes for borer entry holes (clean small round holes with frass) — prune affected canes back to clean white pith.

## May — New growth assessment

### Roses
- Light shaping as new growth fills out.
- Remove any winter-damage you missed.

### Brambles
- Remove primocanes that emerged outside the row (>12 inches from row line).
- Tie new primocanes loosely to trellis as they reach support height.

### Blueberries, cherries
- No pruning. Watch for disease pressure.

## June — Summer tipping starts (brambles)

### Black/purple raspberries
**When primocanes reach 24-30 inches in height**: pinch out the tip of each shoot. This forces lateral branching, which is where next year's fruit forms.

### Blackberries (erect type — Apache, Navaho, Ouachita)
**When primocanes reach target height (30-42 inches depending on cultivar/region)**: tip them. UGA recommends 40-42" for Southeast erects; Clemson recommends 30-36"; UMaine recommends 4 ft for the North.

### Blackberries (semi-erect — Triple Crown)
- Tip primocanes at ~4 ft.

### Blackberries (trailing — some Chester forms)
- Tip 6 inches above the top wire.

### Cherries — STILL DO NOTHING
- Watch the harvest develop. Plan for late-July pruning.

## July — Cherry pruning month + bramble harvest

### Sweet cherries (Kristin, BlackGold) — THE CRITICAL MONTH
**After harvest, on a dry day**:

1. **Sterilize tools** with 70% alcohol (preferred for cherry; bleach corrodes blades).
2. Walk the tree. Identify in priority order: (a) dead/diseased/broken wood, (b) crossing branches, (c) water sprouts and suckers, (d) inward-facing limbs, (e) renewal cuts on ~20% of fruiting wood (oldest first).
3. **For large limbs (≥1"): leave a 6-10 inch stub.** Do NOT flush-cut. Cornell research: stubs become walled-off dead-ends that contain bacterial canker; flush cuts let canker into the trunk.
4. For small cuts: ¼ inch above an outward-facing bud, 45° slope away from the bud.
5. **Don't apply copper or phosphite to wounds.** Cornell tested both; neither provides significant protection. The protection comes from timing, not chemistry.
6. **Cap removal at 20% of total canopy.** More than that triggers excessive water sprouts.
7. **For Kristin specifically**: be especially disciplined. Kristin doesn't have BlackGold's documented bacterial canker resistance; timing is non-negotiable.

This is the single most important pruning event in the user's calendar. Get it done in late July; do not delay into fall.

### Blackberries (post-harvest)
**As soon as the last berries are picked**: cut all spent floricanes to ground level. Floricanes are dying; leaving them harbors anthracnose, cane blight, orange rust, and pests. Bag, burn, or send to municipal yard waste.

### Raspberries (summer-bearing red, post-harvest)
- Cut all canes that produced fruit to ground level. Same logic as blackberries.

### Black/purple raspberries (post-harvest)
- Cut all canes that bore fruit to ground level.

### Blueberries (rabbiteye only, optional)
- Late-July post-harvest pruning is allowed for rabbiteye for size control. Highbush is best left until winter.

## August — Bramble cleanup + fall-bearing raspberry watch

### Brambles
- Continue any post-harvest floricane removal you didn't finish.
- Tie new primocanes to trellis.

### Fall-bearing raspberries
- Fruit forms on the upper third of primocane tips. Leave canes alone.

## September — Fall harvest + light cleanup

### Fall-bearing raspberries
- Harvest the fall crop. Don't prune.

### All plants
- Light cleanup of dead wood and broken branches.
- Inspect cherry trees for canker symptoms (orange-amber gummosis at cuts, sunken bark). If found, prune 4-6" below symptoms; sterilize tools; bag and burn.

## October — Fall protection (NOT structural pruning)

### Roses
- Optional: shorten very tall canes to **3 ft** for wind protection, to prevent winter whip-damage and rocking. **This is wind protection only — not structural pruning.** Save the structural work for spring.
- Apply mulch (~3-4") around the base.

### Brambles, cherries, blueberries
- No pruning. Apply mulch if soil is still warm.

## November-December — Rest

- Tools cleaned, oiled, and stored dry.
- Walk the garden once a month to identify dead wood for spring.
- Order any nursery stock for spring planting.

## Cross-cutting principles

These hold for all five plants. If you're ever in doubt:

1. **Use bypass pruners on live wood.** Anvil pruners crush — fine for dead wood only, never roses or canes.
2. **Cut just outside the branch collar** (NOT into the trunk; NOT a flush cut) — except cherry, where you leave a 6-10" stub.
3. **Sharp tools, clean cuts.** Sharpen at least once a year; wipe blades clean and oil after each session.
4. **Disinfect between bushes** when working on roses or cherries. 70% alcohol is preferred.
5. **Cap removal at 20-25%** of the canopy in one season for trees and shrubs. Knock Outs and primocane-fruiting raspberries are the exceptions (both tolerate full mow-down or 12-18" reset).
6. **Bag and burn diseased prunings.** Do not compost canker-bearing cherry, RRD-suspect rose, or blight-bearing bramble cane.
7. **When in doubt, prune less.** Plants tolerate light annual pruning forever. Hard pruning recovery takes 1-2 years.

## Plant-by-plant cheat sheet

### Kristin and BlackGold sweet cherry
- **WHEN**: Late July, after harvest, on a dry day.
- **WHAT**: Dead/diseased/broken; crossing; water sprouts; renewal of oldest 20% of fruiting wood.
- **HOW**: 6-10" stubs on large cuts (not flush). 70% alcohol between trees.
- **TRAINING**: Modified Central Leader (default) or Kym Green Bush (no-ladder).
- **POLLINATION NOTE**: Kristin needs BlackGold (or another non-Bing-group cultivar) within ~100 ft. Don't prune both heavily in the same year.

### Thornless blackberry
- **WHEN**: Three operations — summer tip (June-July), post-harvest (July-August), late-winter thin and lateral cut (Feb-March).
- **WHAT**: Tip primocanes at 30-42" (cultivar-dependent); remove spent floricanes to ground; thin to 5-7 canes/hill; shorten laterals to ~12 inches.
- **HOW**: Two-wire trellis or V-trellis; tie canes; bypass pruners.

### Knock Out rose
- **WHEN**: Late winter, just as buds swell (forsythia bloom).
- **WHAT**: Annual one-third renewal OR every-2-3-year hard reset to 12-18".
- **HOW**: ¼" above outward-facing bud, 45° slope; sterilize tools between bushes.
- **WATCH**: Rose Rosette Disease — witches' broom, excessive thorns, distorted red leaves. Uproot whole plant if found.

### Raspberry
- **SUMMER-BEARING RED** (Latham, Boyne): late winter thin to 4-5 canes/ft + head 12" above support; post-harvest cut spent floricanes to ground.
- **FALL-BEARING** (Heritage, Caroline): mow ALL canes to ground in late winter for clean fall crop.
- **BLACK/PURPLE** (Jewel, Bristol): summer tip at 24-30"; post-harvest cut spent floricanes; late winter shorten laterals to 12-18".

### Blueberry
- **WHEN**: Late winter while dormant.
- **YEARS 1-2**: Remove all flower buds. No fruit.
- **MATURE**: Remove oldest 1-3 canes per year (~20% canopy). Open the center. Thin twiggy tips.
- **RABBITEYE NUANCE**: Stub-cut at 0-24" (not ground-flush) for replacement-cane vigor; cap height at 6 ft.
