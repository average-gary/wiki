# Demons (Catholic Encyclopedia)

## Nature
- Demons = **fallen angels**: created good, made themselves evil through free choice.
- Same natural powers as faithful angels (pure spirits, no bodies).
- **Lost** supernatural grace, **retained** angelic faculties (intelligence, will, mobility).

## The Fall of Lucifer
- **Lucifer** is the chief of the rebel angels.
- Pride is the canonical sin: refusal to serve.
- Cosmic battle in Revelation 12:7-9 — Michael's angels defeat the dragon and his hosts; cast from heaven.
- Jude 1:6 — "angels who did not keep their proper domain" reserved in chains for judgment.

## Isaiah 14 and Ezekiel 28 — Patristic Reading
- Isaiah 14:12 ("How are you fallen, O Day Star, son of Dawn") was originally addressed to the king of Babylon.
- Ezekiel 28:11-19 ("You were in Eden, the garden of God... a guardian cherub") was originally addressed to the king of Tyre.
- **Patristic + later Catholic commentators** read both with "deeper significance to the fall of the rebel angel" — pride, the cherub-status, the fall from divine intimacy.
- This reading is theologically traditional but not the plain-sense original meaning. Critical scholarship rejects it; popular Christian tradition retains it.

## Named Demons
- **Beelzebub** (Matt 12:24) — "prince of devils," equated with Satan.
- New Advent here doesn't elaborate on Asmodeus (Tobit), Belial (2 Cor 6:15, intertestamental literature), Mammon (Matt 6:24, personification of wealth), or Lilith (Isa 34:14, contested).
- Later Christian demonology (medieval grimoires, *Lesser Key of Solomon*, Wierus's *Pseudomonarchia Daemonum*, 16th-c. Goetia) develops elaborate demon-name lists. These are **esoteric/occult** sources, not Biblical, and a worldbuilder should mine them with awareness that the Church classifies them as superstition or worse.

## Satan's role
"Prince of this world" (John 12:31) until Christ's victory. Governs his fallen hosts; influences but cannot compel humans.
