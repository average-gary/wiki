# The Politics of Jesus — key findings

- **Central thesis**: "Being Christian is a political standpoint." Jesus's life, death, and ethical teachings constitute a coherent political model centered on *nonviolence*, not a spiritualized withdrawal that leaves the political field to "realism" (per Reinhold Niebuhr).
- **The cross as political**: Christ's crucifixion is read not merely as personal-substitutionary atonement but as a *rejection of coercive power structures*. The disciple is called to take up the cross — i.e., to accept suffering rather than inflict it.
- **Anti-Constantinianism**: Yoder's signature critique. The post-Constantinian merger of church and state (4th century onward) is a theological compromise that Anabaptists, Quakers, and the Radical Reformation reject. Just-war theory is, in Yoder's framing, a *Constantinian* accommodation rather than a faithful reading of the gospel.
- **Discipleship as politics**: Following Jesus *necessarily* entails practical commitments to peace and justice in concrete community life — the church *is* the politics, an alternative polity, not a chaplain to the existing one.
- **Reception and caveat**: ~75,000 copies, ten translations, ranked 5th among 20th-century religious books by *Christianity Today*. Hauerwas builds directly on Yoder. **Important caveat**: Yoder was credibly accused of serious sexual misconduct against many women over decades; Mennonite institutions have publicly reckoned with this. The argument can stand on its own merits but the man does not — cite carefully.
