# Augustine
- All magic relies on "cooperation from demons, the henchmen of Satan."
- The category cannot be redeemed by good intent — practice itself is the sin.
- This becomes the dominant patristic / medieval default.

# Aquinas's Three Degrees of Miracle (Summa contra Gentiles)
1. **Above nature** — impossible for nature ever to do (two bodies coincident; sun reversed).
2. **Beyond nature in mode** — outcome nature could produce, but not in this manner (instantly healed sight).
3. **Apart from nature's means** — natural outcome by non-natural means (fever cured by prayer rather than medicine).

These are positive criteria for *miracle* and implicitly negative for *magic*: a magical effect imitates miracle but is sourced demonically.

# The Christian Severity vs. Other Faiths
- Judaism / Islam preserve more nuance about supernatural practice (kabbalah, Solomonic tradition, lawful divination via Urim/Thummim).
- Christianity adopted "far starker" moral absolutism: any practice categorized as magic is intrinsically evil because it allegedly depends on demonic assistance.
- Isidore of Seville's catalog of forbidden practices: divination, enchantment, soothsaying.

# False Signs and Lying Wonders
- Matthew 24:24, 2 Thess 2:9, Revelation 13:13-14: false prophets work real signs by demonic power.
- The biblical worldview *does not deny* that magic can produce real effects — it claims those effects are demonically sourced.

## Implication for a Christian Reskin
This is the core of the **all-magic-is-demonic** approach: in-fiction, any non-divine spellcasting is pact magic, even if the practitioner thinks otherwise. PF2e's four-tradition split collapses into a binary: Divine (Holy Spirit) vs. all others (the lying wonders). A Reformed-leaning table would adopt this; a Catholic table might preserve a "natural-arts-of-creation" loophole (Numenorean smithcraft, Lewis's Merlin-as-vessel).
