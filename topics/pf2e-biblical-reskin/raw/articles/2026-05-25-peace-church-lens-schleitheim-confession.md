# Schleitheim Confession — key findings

- **Seven articles** (Sattler, 1527): (1) believer's baptism, (2) the ban / church discipline, (3) memorial communion for the baptized only, (4) **separation from evil / from worldly institutions**, (5) qualifications and discipline of pastors, (6) **the sword — pacifism and nonresistance**, (7) refusal of oaths.
- **Article 6 ("The Sword")** is direct and load-bearing: "Violence must not be used in any circumstance. The way of nonviolence is patterned after the example of Christ who never exhibited violence." Christians "should not pass judgment in worldly disputes"; serving as magistrate is inappropriate because magistrates "use force or orders force to be used, not acting according to the rules of heaven."
- **Article 4 (Separation)** forbids "participation in the organizations, works, church services, meetings or civil affairs of those who live in contradiction to the commands of God." This applies to *both* Catholics and Protestants — Anabaptism rejects *all* magisterial church-state alignment, not just one.
- **Christian weapons are "spiritual," not worldly** — explicit church/state separation as the *normative* ecclesial stance, not merely a contingent political position. This is theologically prior to debates about just-war theory; the question never gets asked.
- **Believer's baptism + congregational autonomy + nonresistance** form a single integrated package — you cannot adopt the pacifism without also adopting the rejection of state-church alignment, and you cannot adopt either without believer's baptism as the entry rite that makes the church a *voluntary* community of disciples rather than a coextensive-with-society institution.
