# Rapture: The Second Coming

## Identification

Wikipedia confirms a Holistic Design Christian-adjacent RPG: **Rapture: The Second Coming**. This is almost certainly what the prior round's "Book of Days" gap note was reaching for — Holistic Design (the *Fading Suns* publisher) never published a product called "Book of Days." Rapture is the only religiously-themed RPG in their catalog and matches the publisher / era / theme profile.

## Capsule

- **Original**: Quintessential Mercy Studio, 1995, designer William Spencer-Hale.
- **Second edition**: Holistic Design, 2002, produced by Andrew Greenberg, **d20 System** chassis.
- **Genre**: post-apocalyptic religious RPG.
- **Premise** (per *Pyramid* magazine quoted on Wikipedia): "takes its inspiration from various orthodox Christian views of the Apocalypse — the end of the world prophesied in the Bible, particularly in the Book of Revelations."

## Reception

*Pyramid* characterized it as **"notably direct"** among theological games of its era — distinguishing through bluntness rather than esoterica. No mainstream review aggregator (RPGGeek, RPG.net) surfaced substantive review text in this round of digging; the product appears to have been a small-print second edition that did not gain wide critical traction.

## Why this matters for the path

Rapture sits at a different point in the design space than DragonRaid or Testament:

- **Unlike DragonRaid**: Rapture used d20, an existing successful system, rather than building bespoke catechetical mechanics. This avoids the DragonRaid trap.
- **Unlike Testament**: Rapture commits to a specific *theological* reading (orthodox Revelation-driven eschatology) rather than treating Scripture as setting. It's evangelistic-adjacent in framing.
- **Unlike In Nomine**: Rapture takes a stance rather than offering a multi-faith cosmology.

The "notably direct" reception phrase is suggestive — it signals the same risk profile as DragonRaid (will alienate non-Christian gamers) without the pedagogy mechanics (so it's at least a playable d20 game). That it apparently sank without a major print run is itself a data point: even with a strong system chassis, a heavily theology-forward framing limits market reach.
