# Mere Christianity

## The hallway/rooms metaphor
From Lewis's preface (the Wikipedia entry summarizes but the original is the canonical statement): "mere" Christianity is the hall from which doors open into rooms (the denominations). The hall has fires and chairs and meals; one cannot live in it. But one must pass through it to reach a room. Lewis explicitly refuses to recommend a room.

## Included
- Existence of God (moral argument)
- Divinity of Christ (Liar / Lunatic / Lord trilemma)
- Trinity
- Atonement (without specifying a single theory)
- Christian ethics (cardinal + theological virtues)
- Sin, grace, sanctification

## Excluded (deliberately)
- Marian doctrine
- Sacramental theology specifics (number, mode)
- Predestination/free will controversy
- Cessationism vs continuationism
- Ecclesiology (church government)
- Eschatological details (purgatory, millennium)

## Design implications
- **Best for the widest audience.** A "mere Christianity" reskin keeps the Trinity, Christ, angels/demons, heaven/hell, but defers on sacraments, Mary, saints, purgatory, charisms.
- **Risk: thin.** Without a denomination's specifics, the setting may lack the dense imaginative furniture that makes Catholic-Tolkien or Orthodox-Dostoevsky-flavored fiction so vivid.
- **Lewis himself in fiction was less "mere"** than in apologetics — Narnia is recognizably Anglican, with sacramental moments (Aslan's table, the wine-and-bread shape of his sacrifice). The "mere" stance is a pose for shared discourse, not a sustainable creative aesthetic.
- **Use case**: a starter setting bible that flags denomination-specific options as variants.
