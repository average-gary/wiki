# Change Management is complicated, but also entirely predictable

**Author:** u/SatiricalBard (community, not designer)
**Subreddit:** r/Pathfinder2e
**Posted:** ~31 July 2023, two months before the Player Core 1 release.

## Why this is a useful gap-closing source

This is *not* designer commentary. It is a **community summation** of what Paizo's preview communications actually said and what they conspicuously did not say. For a *negative finding* on theological motivation, the community's read of the publicity cycle is corroborating evidence: if the designers had *publicly* given a theological rationale at any point in the long Remaster preview cycle, an active Reddit thread of this kind would have noted it.

## Key relevant quote (verbatim)

> "You'll notice there are no outrage posts about spirit damage, by contrast, **because we all knew that was coming**. People might like or dislike some of the details of how spirit damage, and the holy/unholy/sanctified traits, work. But **nobody is surprised**, and as far as I can see, **only people who wanted to keep mechanical alignment are even talking about it**."

And on the broader pattern of Remaster preview communication:

> "That's why we've seen Remaster previews in the past in which Paizo devs like Jason Bulmahn and Michael Sayre talk about some of the upcoming changes in the Remaster, and some of the thinking behind them."

The author identifies Bulmahn and Sayre (not Bonner) as the designers most active in pre-launch preview communication. These previews focused on **balance and mechanics**, never on theology.

## What this implies for the gap-closing inquiry

1. **Spirit damage and holy/unholy were public knowledge well before Player Core dropped** (Aug 2023 GenCon → Nov 2023 release), but the community treated them as a *foregone licensing-driven swap*, not a debated design philosophy choice.
2. **No designer in the entire Remaster preview cycle gave a theological justification.** If they had, this kind of community essay would have referenced it; instead the essay treats the new categories as obvious mechanical replacements.
3. The only critics of holy/unholy/sanctification were players "who wanted to keep mechanical alignment" — i.e., the debate was framed entirely as *more vs less alignment-style mechanics*, never as *what should replace alignment metaphysically*.

## Significance for the gap-closing claim

This source supports the negative finding from the designer-direct sources ([[2026-05-25-sanctification-designer-commentary-bonner-champion-preview]], [[2026-05-25-sanctification-designer-commentary-loza-divine-mysteries-interview]], [[2026-05-25-sanctification-designer-commentary-paizo-remaster-announcement]]):

> **No public-facing Paizo communication — blog, podcast, AMA, GenCon panel, or interview — has framed the holy/unholy/spirit/vitality/void taxonomy or the sanctification framework in theological, cosmological, or worldbuilding-philosophical terms.** The only stated motivations are (a) OGL-flight and (b) mechanical flexibility / "edicts not unbendable codes."

The wiki's working claim that the PF2e Remaster is *accidentally* monotheism-friendly is therefore **well-grounded in publicly available evidence**, not just an absence of evidence.
