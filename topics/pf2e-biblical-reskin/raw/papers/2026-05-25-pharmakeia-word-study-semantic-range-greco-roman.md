# Pharmakeia — semantic range and cultural matrix

## The word family

- **pharmakon** — drug, potion, remedy, poison, spell-substance (Homeric onward)
- **pharmakeus** — one who handles pharmaka: druggist, poisoner, sorcerer
- **pharmakeia** — the *practice* / *technique* of using pharmaka
- **pharmakos** — the practitioner (NT: Rev 21:8, 22:15); also an unrelated archaic ritual scapegoat usage in classical Greek

## Spectrum of meaning

Ancient Greek "used words like *pharmakeia* to refer to the entire spectrum: from medicines to psychoactives to poisons." The same word covers a healer's tincture and a witch's love-potion. Context selects the sense; the word itself is morally neutral on the surface but pejorative in nearly all NT and LXX contexts.

## Greco-Roman cultural background

- Ancient Mediterranean magic was substance-laden: incense, herbal philtres, potions, ointments. Defixiones (curse tablets) often paired with ingested or applied pharmaka.
- Temple divination — Delphic Pythia included — has been argued to involve pneumatic / chemical alteration of consciousness.
- The pharmakeus / saga / venefica figure in Roman law was prosecuted under poisoning statutes (lex Cornelia de sicariis et veneficis); Roman law fused poisoner and sorcerer as a single category.
- Plato (Laws 933) distinguishes injury "by drugs" from injury "by enchantments," but treats both as pharmakeia and prescribes death for the practitioner.

## Why this matters for the NT

When Paul lists pharmakeia in Gal 5:20 *between* eidololatria (idolatry) and echthrai (hostilities), the surrounding semantic field is **cultic and relational**, not pharmacological per se. Likewise Rev 18:23: Babylon's pharmakeia "deceives all nations" — the offense is **deception via false spiritual technique**, not chemical harm.

Yet the substance-element is not stripped out: the LXX uses pharmak- words to render Egyptian magicians' arts (Exodus 7–8) and Isaiah's Babylonian sorceries (Isa 47:9, 12), where physical implements / muttered spells / philtres are assumed.

## Translational tension

Modern English has no single word that covers "ritual technique combining substance, incantation, and idolatrous intent." Translators pick:
- **"sorcery"** (NIV, ESV, NRSV, KJV) — emphasizes the magical-arts sense
- **"witchcraft"** (KJV at Gal 5:20) — folkloric / pejorative
- **"magic arts"** (NIV at Rev 9:21) — broader
- **"drug-related occult practice"** — periphrastic, captures both poles

The cleanest scholarly gloss: **"illicit ritual technique using drugs and/or incantations to manipulate spiritual powers."**
