# Sheol (Britannica)

## Old Testament Sheol
- Hebrew underworld; appears 66 times in the Hebrew Bible.
- Subterranean, dark, gloomy, "land of deep darkness."
- **Universal destination** — no moral stratification originally; all dead go there (righteous and wicked).
- Vague, sometimes contradictory portrayals; closer to "the grave" than to a punitive hell.

## Second Temple Development (516 BCE - 70 CE)
- Jewish thinkers introduced **compartmentalization** — Sheol gains chambers assigned by moral worth.
- The bosom of Abraham (Luke 16) reflects this stratification.
- The "punishment chamber" of Sheol evolves into **Gehenna**.

## Greek Parallels and Translation
- LXX (Greek Old Testament) translates Sheol as **Hades** — adopting Greek underworld vocabulary.
- **Tartarus** = Greek punishment zone of Hades; appears in 2 Peter 2:4 as the place fallen angels are bound.
- This is why the New Testament has Hades and Tartarus terminology overlapping with Hebrew Sheol.

## Gehenna
- Originally the **Valley of Hinnom** outside Jerusalem — site of child sacrifice to Moloch under apostate kings (2 Kings 23:10).
- Becomes Jewish term for the place of fiery torment for the wicked.
- Jesus's "hell" terminology is overwhelmingly Gehenna, not Sheol/Hades.

## Christian inheritance
Christian "hell" inherits all four streams: Sheol (gloomy underworld), Hades (Greek-coded), Gehenna (fiery punishment), Tartarus (deepest pit for fallen angels). The four are not synonymous in scripture but the popular Christian "hell" collapses them.
