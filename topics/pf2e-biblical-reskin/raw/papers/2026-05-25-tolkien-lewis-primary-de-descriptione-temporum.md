# De Descriptione Temporum (1954)

## Provenance note

Inaugural lecture delivered 29 November 1954 at Cambridge upon Lewis's election to the Chair of Mediaeval and Renaissance Literature. Published in *They Asked for a Paper* (1962) and reprinted in *Selected Literary Essays* (1969). Full-text mirror exists at `homepages.uc.edu/~schaefdw/temporum.html` (University of Cincinnati faculty page); also at Internet Archive djvu mirror. The verbatim passages below are confirmed against both mirrors.

## Where the Great Divide actually falls

Lewis's central thesis: the periodization most scholars use (Antiquity / Dark Ages / Middle Ages / Renaissance / Modern) misplaces the truly significant rupture.

> "It is by these steps that I have come to regard as the greatest of all divisions in the history of the West that which divides the present from, say, the age of Jane Austen and Scott."
> — *De Descriptione Temporum*, as quoted from homepages.uc.edu mirror.

> "Between Jane Austen and us, but not between her and Shakespeare, Chaucer, Alfred, Virgil, Homer, or the Pharaohs, comes the birth of the machines."
> — Same source.

The point is theological-anthropological: Homer and Jane Austen are on the same side of the divide; we are on the other.

## Four reasons for the divide

Lewis grounds the claim in four shifts (per the homepages.uc.edu mirror):

1. **Political:** the shift from rulers seeking to keep subjects quiet to rulers organizing "mass excitement" as the normal organ of political power.
2. **Artistic:** modern poetry's unprecedented difficulty — "seven adults … discussing a very short poem … there is not the slightest agreement among them as to what … it means."
3. **Religious:** the un-Christening of Europe.
4. **The machines.**

## On the un-Christening (the post-Christian, not the pagan)

> "A post-Christian man is not a Pagan; you might as well think that a married woman recovers her virginity by divorce. The post-Christian is cut off from the Christian past and therefore doubly from the Pagan past."
> — *De Descriptione Temporum*, as quoted from homepages.uc.edu mirror.

This is critical for the magic-theology gap: **modern "neo-paganism" is not a return to paganism**; it is something genuinely new and discontinuous, cut off from both Christian and pagan inheritance. The lecture explicitly says modern post-Christianity is *not* equivalent to the pre-Christian world — and so the moral grammar of Acts 19 (burning the magic books) does not simply transfer onto modern occult revivalism without reckoning with this discontinuity.

## On machines and the human mind

> "It alters Man's place in nature."
> — *De Descriptione Temporum* (on the birth of the machines), as quoted from homepages.uc.edu mirror.

> "It is the image of old machines being superseded by new and better ones … this image, potent in all our minds, reigns almost without rival in the minds of the uneducated."
> — Same source.

Lewis's diagnosis: **the machine has become the master metaphor of modern consciousness.** It teaches us that the new is by definition better than the old, that everything is provisional, that conservation matters less than acquisition. This is the *psychological* effect of machinery, distinct from its physical effects.

This converges with Tolkien's Letter 131 collapse of *magic* and *the Machine* into one category: both Inklings see the modern technological imagination as the present-day form of the same disordered will-to-power that ancient sorcery represented.

## On Lewis as "specimen" — the dinosaur passage

> "Speaking not only for myself but for all other Old Western men whom you may meet, I would say, use your specimens while you can. There are not going to be many more dinosaurs."
> — *De Descriptione Temporum*, closing, as quoted from homepages.uc.edu mirror.

Lewis frames himself as a useful informant of a vanished culture — like an Athenian who could explain his own civilization to outsiders. His value as a medievalist is precisely that he reads pre-modern texts "as a native."

## Significance for the magic-theology gap

1. The **modern world is post-Christian, not pagan.** This means a "biblical reskin" of fantasy magic must reckon with the discontinuity Lewis names: today's reader is not a 1st-century Ephesian and not a medieval European. Pharmakeia in Acts 19 lands differently in 2026.
2. **The machine is the master image of modern consciousness** — converging with Tolkien's "magic and machines" being the same vice.
3. **The Great Divide is a Christian periodization argument** — Lewis is making a *theological* claim about modernity being a rupture from the entire premodern world (Christian *and* pagan), not a continuation of it.
4. Lewis offers a hermeneutic: pre-modern texts (including Scripture's magic-condemnation passages) need to be read "as a native" reads them — i.e., from inside the moral grammar of the Old Western world, not transposed onto a post-Christian framework that has different default assumptions about ontology, will, and authority.
