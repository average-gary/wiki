# Letter 131: To Milton Waldman (~1951)

## Provenance and access note

The full letter is in *The Letters of J.R.R. Tolkien* (ed. Carpenter, Houghton Mifflin 1981; rev. exp. 2023). It is not available in primary form online (Internet Archive copy is access-restricted; the Tolkien Estate page only offers a curated synopsis with selected quotes). The verbatim passages below are taken from secondary sources that quote the published text directly. Each has been cross-checked against at least two independent secondary sources (theonering.net, wordonfire.org, livingchurch.org, Tolkien Estate official excerpt).

## Core themes (Tolkien's own framing)

Tolkien identifies the central preoccupations of *The Lord of the Rings* and the legendarium:

> "All this stuff is mainly concerned with **Fall, Mortality, and the Machine**."
> — Letter 131, as quoted by Word on Fire and the Tolkien Estate excerpt.

## On magic and the Machine (one continuous block)

> "By [the use of the word 'magic'] I intend all use of *external* plans or devices (apparatus) instead of development of the inherent inner powers or talents — or even the use of these talents with the corrupted motive of domination; bulldozing the real world, or coercing other wills."
> — Letter 131, as quoted by theonering.net and wordonfire.org.

> "The Machine is our more obvious modern form, though more closely related to Magic than is usually recognized."
> — Letter 131, as quoted on the Tolkien Estate excerpt page.

This is the load-bearing definition. Tolkien collapses *magic* and *the Machine* into a single category: **external apparatus used to bypass inner development and/or to dominate other wills.** This means modern technology and magical sorcery are species of the same vice in his theology of art.

## On the Elves' "magic" as Art (not power)

> "Their 'magic' is Art, delivered from many of its human limitations: more effortless, more quick, more complete (product, and vision in unflawed correspondence). And its object is Art not Power, sub-creation not domination and tyrannous re-forming of Creation."
> — Letter 131, as quoted by theonering.net (with shorter excerpt confirmed by Tolkien Estate).

The scare-quotes around "magic" are Tolkien's own — he is signalling that what readers call "Elven magic" is, in his theology, not magic at all but Art freed from human constraints. The contrast pair is sharp: **Art / sub-creation** vs. **Power / domination / tyrannous re-forming of Creation**.

## On the Enemy's nature

> "The Enemy in successive forms is always 'naturally' concerned with sheer Domination, and so the Lord of magic and machines; but the problem: that this frightful evil can and does arise from an apparently good root, the desire to benefit the world and others — speedily and according to the benefactor's own plans — is a recurrent motive."
> — Letter 131, as quoted by the Tolkien Estate excerpt.

Sauron is "the Lord of magic and machines" (singular category). The corruption is not desire to harm but desire to *help quickly on one's own terms* — i.e. impatience plus self-will.

## On the Ring as externalized power

> "The Ring of Sauron is only one of the various mythical treatments of the placing of one's life, or power, in some external object, which is thus exposed to capture or destruction."
> — Letter 131, as quoted by Word on Fire.

## Significance for the magic-theology gap

1. Tolkien's "magic" is a technical term meaning **externalized, dominating power** — close to what biblical pharmakeia/sorcery names. It is not "supernatural effects" generically.
2. The Elves do not, in Tolkien's own categories, do "magic" at all; they do Art. This is a hard distinction Letter 155 makes more precisely with magia/goeteia.
3. **Magic and the Machine are one category**: this is the move that lets modern industrial technology be theologically diagnosed alongside ancient sorcery.
4. The Enemy is "the Lord of magic and machines" — a single ontological role. Sauron is the *technocratic sorcerer* archetype.
