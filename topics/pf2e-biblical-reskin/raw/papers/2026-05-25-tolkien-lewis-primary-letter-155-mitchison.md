# Letter 155: To Naomi Mitchison (draft, September 1954)

## Provenance note

The letter exists as a draft passage Tolkien did not send (so noted in Carpenter's edition). The full text is in *The Letters of J.R.R. Tolkien*, ed. Humphrey Carpenter, #155. The Internet Archive copy is access-restricted. The verbatim passages below are taken from secondary sources that quote the published text directly, cross-checked across at least three independent sources (phuulishfellow on WordPress, imagicination on WordPress, the Wikipedia "Magic (Middle-earth)" article citing Carpenter 2023 #155, and the scritub.com archive).

## The magia / goeteia distinction (the central passage)

> "I suppose that, for the purposes of the tale, some would say that there is a latent distinction such as once was called the distinction between **magia** and **goeteia**."
> — Letter 155, draft, as quoted by phuulishfellow.

> "**Magia** could be, was, held good (per se), and **goeteia** bad. Neither is, in this tale, good or bad (per se), but only by motive or purpose or use."
> — Letter 155, as quoted by phuulishfellow and the Wikipedia "Magic (Middle-earth)" article (citing Carpenter 2023 #155).

This is the load-bearing move. Tolkien explicitly **rejects** the older Christian/Renaissance moral coding (where magia = legitimate natural philosophy, goeteia = demonic sorcery) and substitutes a motive-based ethics for his story.

## The supremely bad motive: domination of free wills

> "The supremely bad motive is (for this tale, since it is specially about it) **domination of other 'free' wills**."
> — Letter 155, as quoted by phuulishfellow and imagicination.

This is the critical theological hinge. Evil in Middle-earth is not metaphysical (an act of magic per se is not evil); it is **a sin against the free will of others**. This locates Tolkien within an Augustinian-Thomist ethical frame: domination of the will is the core evil, of which both sorcery and tyranny are species.

## How the Enemy uses it (magia + goeteia for domination)

> "His magia he uses to bulldoze both people and things, and his goeteia to terrify and subjugate."
> — Letter 155, as quoted by phuulishfellow.

> "The Enemy's operations are by no means all goetic deceits, but 'magic' that produces real effects in the physical world."
> — Letter 155, as quoted by phuulishfellow.

## How the Elves and Gandalf use it (sparingly, beneficently, artistically)

> "Their **magia** the Elves and Gandalf use (sparingly): a magia, producing real results (like fire in a wet faggot) for specific beneficent purposes."
> — Letter 155, as quoted by phuulishfellow.

> "Their **goetic** effects are entirely artistic and not intended to deceive: they never deceive Elves (but may deceive or bewilder unaware Men)."
> — Letter 155, as quoted by phuulishfellow.

## On both sides living "ordinarily"

> "Both sides live mainly by 'ordinary' means."
> — Letter 155, as quoted by imagicination.

This corrects a common misreading — Middle-earth is *not* high-magic; magic is rare on both sides, and most action is mundane.

## On magic as inherent power, not learned spell

> "It is an inherent power not possessed or attainable by Men as such."
> — Letter 155, as quoted by Wikipedia "Magic (Middle-earth)" citing Carpenter 2023 #155.

> "It is not to be come by by 'lore' or spells; but is in an inherent power."
> — Letter 155, as quoted on lotrdailyquotes (per phuulishfellow's link survey).

This is essential: in Tolkien's cosmology, magic is **ontological**, not procedural. It belongs to a creature's nature (Elves, Maiar, Dwarves to a degree), not to a learnable technology of spells. This is the inverse of the D&D "spell list" model that PF2e inherits.

The qualification (per Wikipedia article): Tolkien noted Númenóreans used "spells" in making swords, suggesting Men can borrow a participation in magic through making/craft, but not natively wield it.

## On Tolkien's own caution

> "I am afraid I have been far too casual about 'magic' and especially the use of the word…"
> — Letter 155 opening of the relevant passage, as referenced in multiple Tolkien-fan citations on this letter (phuulishfellow attributes it; opening sentence varies slightly across sources).

> "I do not intend to involve myself in any debate whether 'magic' in any sense is real…"
> — Letter 155, as quoted by imagicination.

## Significance for the magic-theology gap

1. **Magia/goeteia distinction** is *not* used in the traditional Christian sense by Tolkien — he explicitly says neither is good or bad per se in his world.
2. The **moral test is motive: domination of free wills.** This aligns with biblical/Thomist sorcery ethics (1 Sam 28; Acts 8 Simon Magus; Aquinas ST II-II Q95-96) where the evil is in the *coercion of order* and *trafficking with hostile spirits*, not in any "supernatural effect" itself.
3. **Inherent power, not learnable technique** — Tolkien's Elves/Wizards have magic as *what they are*, not as a skill set. This is the deepest contrast with PF2e's spellbook/spell-list model and with biblical pharmakeia (which is about *practitioners* engaged in technique).
4. **Both sides live ordinarily** — magic is rare and exceptional, not pervasive infrastructure.
5. The Enemy's use of "magia to bulldoze" and "goeteia to terrify and subjugate" maps precisely to the biblical complaint against sorcery: it is power deployed *against the freedom of persons.*
