# Aquinas, ST II-II, Q.95 — De divinationibus

## Article structure (8 articles)

1. Whether divination is a sin?
2. Whether it is a species of superstition?
3. Of the species of divination.
4. Divination by demons.
5. Divination by the stars.
6. Divination by dreams.
7. Divination by auguries, omens.
8. Divination by lots.

## Q.95 A.1 Respondeo — Why divination is sin

> "If anyone presume to foreknow or foretell such like future things by any means whatever, except by divine revelation, he manifestly usurps what belongs to God."

The future-as-such (contingent particulars) is **proper to God's knowledge**. Predicting from real natural causes (eclipses, weather) is licit; predicting contingent particulars without divine revelation usurps God's prerogative.

## Q.95 A.2 — Divination as superstition

> "All divination results from the demons' operation."

Either by explicit invocation, OR demons "thrust themselves into futile searchings of the future, in order to entangle men's minds with vain conceits."

## Q.95 A.3 — The three principal species of divination

1. **Express invocation of demons**: necromancy, prestigiation, dream-divination (when sought from demons), pythonic divination, geomancy, hydromancy, aeromancy, pyromancy.
2. **Observation without express invocation**: astrology, bird-augury, omens, chiromancy (palmistry).
3. **Active sortilege**: drawing lots to discover hidden knowledge.

## Q.95 A.5 — Astrology distinction (load-bearing)

Astrology is **licit** when it forecasts effects with real natural causation (eclipses, tides, weather affecting bodies). It is **superstitious** when used to predict "casual or fortuitous future events, or to know with certitude future human actions" — because human will is free and contingent particulars are God's.

## Q.95 A.8 — Lots

Sortilege is permissible only under "urgent necessity" with reverence toward divine providence (e.g., dividing goods justly, ecclesiastical decisions per Acts 1). Forbidden when applied "to worldly matters and to the vain things of this life."

# Aquinas, ST II-II, Q.96 — De superstitionibus observantiarum

## Article structure (4 articles)

1. Observances of the magic art.
2. Observances directed to the alteration of bodies.
3. Observances practiced in fortune-telling (e.g., to detect a thief).
4. Wearing divine words at the neck (amulets, phylacteries).

## Q.96 A.1 Respondeo — Magic art

Magic art is "unlawful, because the means it employs for acquiring knowledge have not in themselves the power to cause science." The signs/figures employed are **"empty signs"** constituting "a kind of agreement or covenant made with the demons."

## Q.96 A.2 — Bodily alteration through observances

Practices qualify as superstitious when "they are employed for the purpose of producing those effects, not as causes but only as signs," thereby entering "compact by tokens entered into with the demons."

The **causal-vs-significatory test**: if the means really has natural power to produce the effect (medicine, herbs), it is licit. If the means is symbolic/sign-based without natural causal power, the only way it could "work" is via demonic agreement.

## Q.96 A.3 — Fortune-telling observances

> "Superstitious and unlawful... remains of idolatry."

Lacking "reason and art," they presuppose demonic interpretation of the sign.

## Q.96 A.4 — Sacred words at the neck

Lawful only if (a) the words contain "nothing vain" — only canonical Scripture; (b) no "certain written characters, except the sign of the Cross"; (c) no vain observance about how/when worn. Otherwise the amulet itself becomes superstitious.

## The Aquinas test (load-bearing for game design)

For any practice claimed to produce an effect, ask:
1. Does the means have **natural causal power** for the effect? → licit (medicine, craft, real engineering).
2. Is the effect produced by **God's revelation/grace**? → licit (prayer, sacraments, miracle).
3. Is the means a **sign or token without natural causal power**? → superstitious; "works" only by demonic compact (explicit or tacit).
