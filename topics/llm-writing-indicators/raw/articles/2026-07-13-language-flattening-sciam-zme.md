# Language flattening — Scientific American + ZME Science

**Quality: 4/5.** Reader-friendly reporting on Yakura et al. (Max Planck).

## Findings

- **Rising "GPT words" in human speech:** delve, realm, meticulous, boast, swift, inquiry.
- Origin anecdote (Yakura): "realized I was using 'delve' more."
- **Prestige-copying mechanism** (Brinkmann): "We're more likely to copy what someone else is doing if we perceive them as being knowledgeable or important."
- **Quantified magnitude (ZME):** "annual increases of 25–50%" for GPT words in academic talks; steepest in science/education podcasts, **flat in Religion & Spirituality and Sports** — adoption is domain-dependent.
- "We train the machines, they talk back to us, and then we talk like them."

## Why it matters for a reviewer

The tell-words are bleeding into human language at 25–50%/year, so a word like "delve" in a 2026 document is weaker evidence than it was in 2023. The broader "language flattening" discourse (The Atlantic's "Great Language Flattening," April 2025; New Yorker's Chayka on homogenized thought) is the cultural context: as everyone's prose converges on the AI mean, lexical tells lose power and only deeper signals (no original ideas, no lived detail) survive.
