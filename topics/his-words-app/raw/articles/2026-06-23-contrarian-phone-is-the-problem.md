## The Critique

**Core argument**: If the problem is that Christians are drowning in digital distraction and losing time to God's Word, the answer is *structural absence from the device*, not adding another overlay. Andy Crouch (_Tech-Wise Family_) frames this as the difference between "technology as a tool" (which we can theoretically optimize) vs. "technology as a way of life" (which reshapes desire itself). Interrupting TikTok with a verse doesn't change what the user *wants to do*—it just introduces friction before returning to the same pattern.

**The evidence**:
- Crouch distinguishes between "wise tech use" (family covenants, time boundaries, communal rhythms) and "app-based solutions" (which atomize the problem and individualize it, making the phone the site of redemption rather than eliminating it from key moments).
- Cal Newport's _Digital Minimalism_ cites studies showing that willpower-based resistance (like pausing for a verse) is depleted quickly; sustainable change requires *elimination*, not interruption.
- Tony Reinke in _12 Ways Your Phone Is Changing You_ explicitly warns against "baptizing the smartphone" with Scripture—adding Bible content doesn't address the underlying neurological reshaping the device creates.

**Why this matters for His Words**: If Reinke is right that the phone fundamentally rewires attention and desire (independent of content), then "pause and read Scripture" still leaves the user in the device's neurological grip. The interruption might be *theologically* coherent but *neurologically* ineffective.

## The Steelman: What Would Need to Be True for His Words to Overcome This?

His Words would need to demonstrate:

1. **Measurable re-orientation of desire**, not just compliance. Does the interruption (over weeks/months) actually shift what users *want* when they pick up their phone? Do they start reaching for Scripture without the app's prompting?

2. **Comparison to hard-boundary alternatives** (paper Scripture, offline family Bible time, phone-free Sabbaths). If 90 min/week of intentional, offline Bible study + a phone-free evening outperforms 15 min/day scattered through interruptions, His Words loses on the theological grounds that it's optimizing for the wrong site (the phone instead of away from it).

3. **Accountability data**: Do users report greater personal discipline and reduced social-media time? Or just more compliance with a religious prompt? (The distinction matters: one reshapes character; the other adds guilt to existing patterns.)

## Counter-Differentiation

**What an alternative product shape might do**:
- **Family/accountability covenant tool** (not an app, or a very thin app): SMS reminders to check in with a Bible-reading partner, not verse interruptions. The accountability is *human*, not algorithmic.
- **"Phone-free time" enforcer**: Hard schedule (6-7 PM no phone, family dinner + paper Bible). Uses API to enforce OS-level restrictions, not to add content.
- **Paper Bible + GPS geofencing**: A physical Bible placed at strategic locations (desk, car, kitchen) with an app that reminds you "There's a Bible by your coffee" rather than interrupting to *show* you Scripture.

## Theological Stake

If Crouch + Newport are right, His Words is *optimizing the wrong variable*. The problem isn't "not enough Scripture on my phone"; it's "my phone is the problem." Redeeming screen time by *adding* redemptive content is like trying to solve alcohol addiction with better wine. The differentiation isn't in the interruption rhythm—it's in whether the product accepts or rejects the phone as the site of discipleship.

**His Words must answer**: Is the phone a tool for spiritual formation, or is its very presence in your pocket the obstacle?
