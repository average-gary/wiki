# Implementation Intentions and Habit Substitution

## Citations
- Gollwitzer, P. M. (1999). Implementation intentions: Strong effects of simple plans. *American Psychologist*, 54(7), 493–503. https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.54.7.493
- Gollwitzer, P. M., & Sheeran, P. (2006). Implementation intentions and goal achievement: A meta-analysis of effects and processes. *Advances in Experimental Social Psychology*, 38, 69–119.
- Pinder, C., Vermeulen, J., Cowan, B. R., & Beale, R. (2018). Digital behaviour change interventions to break and form habits. *ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction*, 25(3), 1–66.
- Wood, W. (2019). *Good Habits, Bad Habits: The Science of Making Positive Changes That Stick.* (Trade book summarizing Wood lab's empirical work.)

## Theory in one sentence
An *implementation intention* is a pre-committed if-then plan: "If situation X occurs, then I will perform behavior Y." This delegates control of behavior from conscious goal pursuit to **automatic environmental cue → response** linkage.

## Empirical findings

### Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006) meta-analysis
- **Studies pooled**: 94 independent studies.
- **Total N**: ~8,000 participants across diet, exercise, study habits, addiction recovery, screening adherence.
- **Effect size**: **d = 0.65** (medium-to-large) for goal attainment when implementation intentions were added to mere goal intentions.
- **Robustness**: Effect held across populations (clinical and non-clinical), behavior types, and timeframes (single-shot to multi-month).

### Sheeran, Webb & Gollwitzer (2005) "Interplay" paper
- Implementation intentions are **most effective when goal commitment is already high**. People who don't care about the goal don't benefit much; people who do, benefit a lot.

### Pinder et al. (2018) — TOCHI digital review
- Reviewed digital behavior-change apps; found *very few* embed implementation-intention scaffolding correctly. Most apps offer "set a goal" without the "if-then" trigger structure. Those that do (e.g., context-aware notifications fired by sensor cues) outperform those that don't.

### Wendy Wood lab (multiple papers, 2002-2019)
- ~43% of daily behavior is habitual, performed automatically in response to context cues.
- Habit formation requires **consistent context-cue → behavior pairing** repeated until automaticity emerges (~66 days median per Lally et al. 2010, with high variance).
- **Habit substitution**: easier to replace an existing habit's *response* while keeping the same *cue*, than to extinguish the habit entirely.

## Applicability to His Words' interruption-rhythm hypothesis
**Strong evidence** for the substitution model:
- The user's existing habit: *cue (boredom / phone in hand) → open Instagram → scroll → dopamine.*
- His Words intervenes at the **response stage**: the user has already opened Instagram, but at minute N a Scripture verse appears. The cue (boredom) is unchanged, but the response is partially substituted (scroll → read verse → reflect).
- This is exactly the "cue stays, response changes" pattern Wood recommends as easier than full extinction.
- The interruption *is* the implementation-intention trigger: "If I'm in Instagram for 5 minutes, then I read a verse." The user pre-commits at install/configuration time.

## Effect-size translation
A medium-to-large d = 0.65 from implementation intentions, *combined with* the ~50% reduction from One Sec-style frictions, suggests stacked interventions could be substantial. But **only if** the user's underlying goal commitment is high — i.e., they actually want to spend less time scrolling and more time with Scripture. The audience for His Words self-selects for that commitment, which strengthens the predicted effect size.

## Design recommendations transferable
1. **Make the trigger explicit and pre-committed.** During onboarding, have the user write or affirm: "When I'm in [app] for [N] minutes, I want to pause for Scripture." This *is* the implementation intention.
2. **Use a salient, consistent cue.** The same visual treatment for every pause builds the cue → response link.
3. **Automate the cue.** Don't rely on the user to remember; the app fires the cue automatically. (Implementation intentions only work if the cue actually triggers.)
4. **Substitute, don't merely interrupt.** The verse should feel like a *meaningful alternative response*, not just an obstacle. Reflection prompt + verse > verse alone.
5. **Filter to high-commitment users.** Onboarding should re-confirm goal commitment; users who don't commit will not benefit much.

## Evidence rating
**Strong** — foundational social-psychology theory with robust meta-analytic support and direct HCI applications. The strongest theoretical scaffolding for the His Words model.
